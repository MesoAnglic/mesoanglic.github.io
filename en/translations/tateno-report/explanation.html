Explanations and Remarks

for Observations by Pilot Balloon

For observing by pilot balloon we almost always use the two-theodolite method. We have two baselines, 1000 m and 2267 m long. The long baseline is used for fine weather, and the short one for cloudy weather. In the following tables we give only the results of the two-theodolite method. Sometimes one of the two observation posts loses the balloon from view, while the other continues the observation. In that case one first computes the ascending speed of the balloon by the two-theodolite method, using the observations in the lower part, and computes the direction and speed of the high part by the further observations of the one theodolite; but the result is not as inaccurate as that of a wholly absolute one-theodolite method. To indicate results computed in this way the sign ∗ is used.

The table contains the time at which the balloon was sent up, the height of the balloon, and the direction and speed of the wind corresponding to the minutes after release. The direction of the wind is expressed in degrees, north as 0°, east as 90°, south as 180° and west as 270°. The height above the sea is expressed in metres, and the speed in metres per second. The direction and speed of the wind for the height of 40 m are according to the anemometer, which is on the tower of the office. Of course the anemometer is 15 m above the ground, and 40 m above the sea. The amount of cloud is according to the scale 0—10, and the forms of cloud are according to the international symbols.
