On the ascending speed of the pilot balloon

by W. Oishi.

1. Preface.

In the one-theodolite method of pilot-balloon observations, the ascending speed of the balloon forms the basis for computing the direction and the speed of the wind. From this point it is absolutely necessary to have certain knowledge of the ascending speed of the balloon. The ascending speed is computed, for example,

1) according to Dines

v = 81 · A3/5 / (A+B)2/5     (D)

2) according to "the signal corps" of the U.S.A.

v = 72 · {A5 / (A+B)2}0.08     (U)

In the formulas stated above, v means the ascending speed of the pilot balloon expressed in m/min, A the ascending force of the balloon, B the weight of the balloon, and A and B are both expressed in grams. We aim in this work to find out whether the ascending speed of the balloon computed from the formulas truly coincides with the real speed. To learn the real speed we used the two-theodolite method, and took two baselines, which were 1000 and 2269 metres long. The hydrogen for inflating the balloon was bought from the Yokohama Chemical Industry Co. until the end of 1923, and afterwards from the Hotogaya Soda Factory. The total number of balloons sent up, used in this work, is 1030, and they were made in the following factories:

1. Balloon factory in Osaki near Tokyo.(J)
2. Sterling Rubber Co., Canada.(K)
3. Continental Rubber Co., Germany.(G)

Hereafter we shall use the signs (J), (K) and (G) to denote the respective types of balloon.

18. Convection and turbulence.

In chapter 13 we already noted that the ascending speed of the balloon is greatest at 14ʰ and smallest at 6ʰ. From this relation one might think that the speed depends entirely on the air convection due to heat. However, we also noted, in chapter 14, that the speed is great in winter and small in summer. This is quite contrary to the convection theory; for the convection must be greatest in summer and smallest in winter. We now try to find an explanation otherwise, and among other things investigate how it relates to the wind. Table XVII shows us that the ascending speed of the balloon is smallest for calm weather, increases with the strengthening of the wind, and reaches the maximum at a wind of 3–4 m/sec. The facts stated above appear to be results of the turbulent agitation of the air, which also exists, as Wenger already noted earlier, in the free air. With turbulence the air resistance decreases at a certain strength of the wind, and consequently the ascending speed increases. The topographic influence may also be present at times. Although our observatory lies wholly in the broad field, there is nevertheless 3 metres of height difference around us, and sometimes balloons may be carried up too rapidly by the strong wind. In a word, the ascending speed of the balloon increases near the ground because of convection, turbulence and topographic influence.

19. Conclusion.

1) The real ascending speed of the pilot balloon was observed by the two-theodolite method. The total number of observations is 1030.

2) Balloons with weights of 10–120 gr. were used.

3) Observations with ascending speeds of 100–350 m/min were made.

4) The real speed was compared with the values computed by the formulas of Dines and "the signal corps" of the U.S.A.

5) The real speed is generally greater than the computed one.

6) The real speed almost coincides with the computed one at the speed of 100 m/min, and becomes greater by 20% than the computed one at the speed of 200 m/min.

7) To conform to the result of the observations the new formula

v = 74.6 · {A / (A+B)2/3}1/1.53

was made. It is usable for computing the mean speed in the air-layer of 0–3 km.

8) To reduce the value computed by the above formula to the mean for the air-layer of 0–10 km one should multiply the value by the factor 1000/1071 = 0.934.

9) The ascending speed of the balloon is greatest at 14ʰ and smallest at 6ʰ.

10) The ascending speed is great in winter and small in summer.

11) The ascending speed is very great near the ground, and decreases upward more and more, but at the height of 8 km it begins to increase.

12) The near-ground increase of the ascending speed is due to convection and turbulence, and a little to topographic influence.
