Description

of the Aerological Observatory of Tateno.

1. History.

For more than twenty years the Central Meteorological Observatory of Japan intended to found an aerological observatory, but the government did not readily agree. First, on 26 August 1920, the present Aerological Observatory of Tateno was inaugurated by Imperial Ordinance, and W. Oishi, then a meteorologist of the central observatory, was appointed director of the aerological observatory. Owing to a lack of money and personnel, the installations and the observations of the observatory were not complete during the first few years. Ordinary meteorological observations, which we call near-ground observations to distinguish them from the aerological observations, began on 1 November 1920; observations by pilot balloon, however, began in April 1921, and observations by kite and captive balloon from the end of 1922.

The observatory is situated 60 km northeast of Tokyo. One uses the Joban Line railway, which departs from Ueno station in Tokyo. From Tokyo one first travels to the town of Tsuchiura, reached by train after two hours. The observatory is a further 10 km southwest of Tsuchiura, from where one continues the journey by public motor-car and after 15 minutes reaches the observatory. (See illustration I)

3. Surroundings of the Observatory.

The whole area of the observatory is 528,095 square metres, i.e. more than half a square kilometre. The observatory lies in a wholly open field. The highest part is only 26 m above the sea, and the lowest 22 m. The central part of the field forms a long level stretch running from SSW to NNE. The surroundings of the observatory consist chiefly of woods, farms and rice fields. This region is the northeastern part of the Kanto plain, which is the largest plain in Japan. The nearby mountain range is Tsukuba, which is situated 20 km north of the observatory, and the highest peak of the mountain is only 876 m above the sea. (See illustrations I & II)

4. Buildings of the Observatory.

The buildings of the observatory are made chiefly of wood. The main building is the office, which has an anemometer tower. The kite house, from which kites and captive balloons are sent up for aerological observations, is situated in the centre of the field, and the ground surface there is 26 m above the sea. In the balloon house captive balloons and kites are stored. In the machine house there are an electric generator, accumulators and a suction-gas engine. Other buildings are the workshop, the laboratory and the dwellings. The following table shows the area of the buildings.

5. Roads in the Observatory.

The roads in the observatory are orthogonal or radial. For the orthogonal system two directions are used, N–S and W–E, and for the radial system the 16 directions N, NNE, NE, ENE, E, ESE, SE, SSE, S, SSW, SW, WSW, W, WNW, NW and NNW. Around the office and dwellings an orthogonal system is adopted, and from the kite house 16 radial roads run out, with the house as the centre. All sides of the buildings are constructed exactly parallel to the direction N–S or W–E. Such systems of roads and buildings are very convenient for meteorological observations, in which one must always consider the directions of various phenomena. (See illustration II)

7. Arrangement of the Observatory.

1) Near-ground observations. We use the new name "near-ground observation" to denote the ordinary meteorological observation and to distinguish it from the aerological observation. These near-ground observations are made absolutely, i.e. by eye, three times a day at 6ʰ, 14ʰ and 22ʰ, and hourly results are computed by means of the recording instruments. (See illustration VII)

2) Observations by pilot balloon. A pilot balloon is a small spherical rubber balloon about 50–100 cm in diameter. The pilot balloon is inflated with hydrogen and grows larger as it ascends. The pilot balloon carries neither an aeronaut nor an instrument; it is only the balloon itself. It travels vertically at an almost constant speed, and horizontally with the wind. If one determines the position of the pilot balloon at each moment, one can compute the direction and speed of the wind. To measure the position of the pilot balloon a theodolite, made especially for the purpose, is used. With the theodolite one observes the azimuth and altitude angle of the pilot balloon each minute. There are two methods, namely the one-theodolite method and the two-theodolite method. The first method is simple, but one must assume the ascending speed of the balloon, which is not accurate. In the second a baseline is needed, at both end points of which theodolites are placed. We almost always use the two-theodolite method.

3) Observations by kite. The kite for aerological observations is of a rectangular parallelepiped shape, and has a volume of about 1.8 × 2.3 × 0.6 m³. The kite is tethered by a steel wire of 0.7–1.1 mm diameter. This wire is wound around a cylindrical reel. The kite is sent up with an instrument, which automatically records air pressure, air temperature, humidity and wind. To send up the kite, one first carries the kite horizontally over the ground a few hundred metres, and then rapidly draws in the wire by an electric motor. Now the kite begins to rise and then ascends higher and higher by the strong wind in the upper air. During the ascent of the kite the force of the wind unwinds the wire, and during the descent the force of the electric motor winds it in. (See illustration VIII)

4) Observations by captive balloon. When the wind is weak one cannot send up the kite, and a captive balloon is used for aerological observations. The present balloon of the observatory is of spherical shape, and its volume is about 40 m³. The balloon is filled with hydrogen and sent up with an automatically recording instrument. It is tethered by the same steel wire used for the kite.

5) Observations by sounding balloon. A sounding balloon is a spherical rubber balloon of 1–2 m diameter. The balloon is inflated with hydrogen and sent up with an automatically recording instrument, which weighs about 500 grams. During the ascent the balloon grows larger, finally bursts, and descends. Now someone finds the recording instrument, which falls from the balloon to the ground, and we examine the record of the instrument. By the instrument one can observe atmospheric pressure, temperature and humidity.

6) Photographic observations of clouds. We intend to make photographic observations of clouds to measure upper-air wind and structure. But owing to the lack of personnel the observations are not yet complete.

7) Machines. There is a suction-gas engine of 30 horsepower and an electric generator of 20 k.w. We charge 72 accumulators (each accumulator has a capacity of 218 amp. h.), and the electric energy is used for the electric motors in the kite house and workshop, and also for the electric lamps in all the buildings.

8) Hydrogen. Hydrogen for the pilot balloon, captive balloon and sounding balloon is bought from the soda-manufacturing company in Hotogaya, from which the hydrogen is transported as a compressed gas.
