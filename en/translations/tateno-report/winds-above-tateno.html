The Wind above Tateno

by W. Oishi.

1. Preface.

Aerological observations by pilot balloon began at our observatory in April 1921. At first we used the one-theodolite method. At the end of 1921 we made a baseline 1001.5 metres long, and began the observations with two theodolites. Furthermore, from April 1923 we made a longer baseline (2264 m). From that time we used the long baseline for fine weather and the short one for cloudy weather. The baselines changed their length slightly a few times; now they are 1000 m and 2267 m.

The following investigations are based on the pilot-balloon observations made during the two years from March 1923 to February 1925. The total number of observations is 1288. All observations are by the two-theodolite method. The direction and speed thus computed have no relation to the ascending speed of the balloon, which one must assume in the one-theodolite method. The rubber balloons used by us were made in the following factories:

  1. Balloon factory in Osaki near Tokyo.
  2. Sterling Rubber Co., Canada.
  3. Continental Rubber Co., Germany.

On the weight, ascending force and ascending speed of the balloon we are informed in detail in the work „On the ascending speed of the pilot balloon.“

The work of the pilot-balloon observations is not without hardship. The observations sometimes last more than two hours. One must often endure the telescopic observations under the burning summer sunshine. One must also struggle against the bitter cold of winter. This work might not have come into being without the tireless energy of the young, lively observers, to whom on this occasion I express my heartfelt thanks.

Classification of the four seasons. We use the following classification:—

Spring:March, April, May.
Summer:June, July, August.
Autumn:September, October, November.
Winter:December, January, February.

The height above the sea is expressed in metres or kilometres, and the speed in metres per second. The direction of the wind is expressed in degrees (N=0°, E=90°, S=180°, W=270°). The direction and speed of the wind for the height of 0.04 km are according to the anemometer, which is situated 40 m above the sea.

2. Speed of the wind.

Method of computation. In Table I the speed of the wind is given at every 100 metres of altitude. The material is not sufficient to make a monthly mean, and here we present only the seasonal mean. Separate means for 6ʰ, 10ʰ and 14ʰ are also prepared. As one sees in the table, the vertical change of the wind speed is too great, and we recompute to smooth the change by the following formula:

vn′ = (vn−1 + vn + vn+1) / 3,

where vn−1, v and vn+1 are the mean speeds of the wind at the heights of n−1, n and n+1 hectometres respectively; further, vn′ is the new value of the speed for the altitude n. The result thus recomputed is shown by Table II, in which separate means for 6ʰ, 10ʰ and 14ʰ are also presented. A daily variation is not visible higher than 2000 m, and the means for the various hours are not made for the upper air, so here we present only the result for the lower air. Figures I, II and III graphically show the figures given in Table II.

Annual mean speed of the wind. Figure I shows us well that the mean wind is weakest at the ground and grows stronger and stronger in the upper air. One sees that the wind blows 3 metres per second at the ground, but the speed becomes 5.4 metres per second at the height of 300 m. From that height up to 1.5 km the increase of the speed is not great, and afterwards it grows again. However, in the air higher than 6 km the speed scarcely changes. The maximum speed, which appears at the height of 6.5 km, is 24.4 metres per second.

Seasonal change of the wind speed. As fig. II and tab. II show us, on the whole from the ground to the height of 10 km the winter wind is stronger than the summer one. Generally, in all four seasons the speed of the wind increases rapidly for every 500 metres upward from the ground. The cause is doubtless that the friction of the ground against the air and other obstacles has scarcely any influence at the height of 500 m. At the height of 1000 m the wind often changes direction, i.e. that height often forms the boundary between the upper and lower wind, on account of which one often sees a weak wind at this height. In this way the general increase of the wind almost halts at that altitude.

In winter the monsoon is a WNW wind, and its direction is almost the same as the upper-air westerly wind; further, the barometric gradient is very great in this season. Both these causes make a strong winter wind, all the way from the ground to the upper air. In summer, by contrast, the monsoon has a direction entirely different from the upper circulating wind, and the upper wind itself is weak and variable. Thus the summer wind is weak everywhere at all levels of the atmosphere. (See also the section on the direction of the wind.)

Daily variation of the wind speed. To know in detail about the daily variation one must make observations many times a day; but we have only three observations made at 6ʰ, 10ʰ and 14ʰ, and the material is not sufficient. Nevertheless one can by this see the general aspect of the daily variation; since the air temperature is minimum at 6ʰ and maximum at 14ʰ, daytime and nighttime radiation has the greatest influence at these two hours, and we can consider that 14ʰ represents day and 6ʰ night as regards the daily variation.

In Table II and Figure III we see that the wind at the ground is strongest at 14ʰ and weakest at 6ʰ; however, at the small height of 100 m the speed for 6ʰ already equals that for 10ʰ, and at the height of 300 m the speed for 6ʰ is greater than that for 10ʰ and equals that for 14ʰ. At the level higher than 400 m the wind at 6ʰ is stronger than at 14ʰ. In this way one can perceive the daily variation up to 2000 m. At a higher place the number of observations is too few to establish the very small daily variation there.

It is well known that at the ground the wind is strong by day and weak by night, while on the mountain we have quite the opposite — a strong night wind and a weak day wind. On Mount Tukuba (876 m above the sea) and even on Fuzi (3778 m) one can easily perceive the fact. However, in the free air one cannot establish the phenomenon at as high an atmosphere as on Mount Fuzi. Of course the phenomenon is caused by the mixing of the upper and lower air during the day, and the effect must be very marked on the mountain because of the strong rising air along the mountain slope.

3. Direction of the wind.

Method of computation. To investigate the vertical distribution of the wind direction, we first group from the material the directions corresponding to the same heights, separated at every hundred metres, and we compute the vector sum of the directions observed at the same height. Table III is the result, which gives the seasonal means separated for 6ʰ, 10ʰ and 14ʰ. To smooth the abrupt vertical changes of the direction we again compute a vector sum by the following formula:

r̄n′ = r̄n−1 + r̄n + r̄n+1,

where rn−1, rn and rn+1 are the mean directions at the heights of n, n−1 and n+1 hectometres respectively; further, rn′ is the new value of the direction at the height of n hectometres. Table IV gives the result. Separate means for 6ʰ, 10ʰ and 14ʰ appear here only up to 2000 m, since higher up the daily variation is not perceptible. Figures IV and V graphically show the figures given in Table IV.

Below 2 km, the monsoon and local influences are noticeable, and the particular characters of all the seasons appear. At the ground the mean direction is NE in spring, SE in summer, NW in autumn and WNW in winter. However, these differing directions at the ground all become W in the upper air, and the layer from the ground to 2 km is in the changeable state leading to the constant direction.

In all seasons the wind turns to the right upward from the ground. In spring, summer and autumn the wind becomes WSW already at the height of 1 km, but a true westerly wind we see first at the height of 2 km. In winter the wind turns to the right from the ground up to the height of 400 m, but higher up it turns to the left and reaches the layer of westerly wind already at the height of 1 km. Daily variation of the wind direction. As we said earlier, we assume that the day is represented by the observation at 14ʰ and the night by that at 6ʰ, and we make out the general aspect of the daily variation of the wind direction. Table IV and Figure V show us that we can perceive the daily variation near the ground because of local and seasonal influence. In all seasons, except summer, the daily variation is not noticeable above 1400 m. In summer, however, the variation is distinguishable at the height of 2 km and even in the higher air. But we are not bold enough to assert that the variation is wholly diurnal; since the summer upper wind is not constant as in other seasons, and moreover the non-uniformity of the observations produces a difference between the observations at the various hours.

Frequency of the wind. Until now we have spoken only of the mean direction of the wind, but often we can be deceived because we are too trusting of the mean. For example, at Tateno in summer easterly and southerly winds are very frequent, but the mean wind, southeasterly, is in reality rare. In spring, at 1 km above Tateno, the mean wind direction is WSW, which in reality is rare, although SSW and WNW are truly frequent. For this reason we give Tables V–XII, which show the number of wind observations, separating them into 16 directions. Below 2 km, the statistics are also made separating for 6ʰ, 10ʰ and 14ʰ. Figures VI–XIII graphically show the figures given in Tables V–XII. As Tables IX–XII and Figures X–XIII show us, below 2 km and especially at the ground, the wind changes variously according to the various hours and various seasons. About the wind at the ground we shall write in detail in the following number of this report. The influence of the ground decreases more and more upward, and generally we have a westerly wind in the air higher than 2 km. In the seasons except summer, there we see almost only a westerly wind; in summer, however, other winds are not lacking and often even the easterly wind is observed.

On the whole, in all seasons the daily variation of the direction is distinguishable up to 2 km. The monsoon also halts at that altitude. In winter a WNW monsoon blows near the ground, the westerly wind becomes more and more abundant above the height of 1.5 km, and it is most observable first at the height of 2 km. In the air higher than 2 km, the wind is almost always westerly. Only in summer is an easterly wind not lacking at the height of 10 km and higher. There is no doubt that the westerly wind in the upper air is the general circulating air-current of the world. What, then, does the summer easterly wind in the upper air mean? In the isobaric chart for July at the height of 8000 m, presented by Shaw*, we see that the air pressure generally falls from the equator to the pole, and besides this there are two great high-pressure areas. One high-pressure area extends from Africa to Mongolia across Arabia and India, and the other from Mexico to the western Atlantic. If the high-pressure area over India and Mongolia happened to extend farther east to China and Japan, and we are at its southeastern edge, we might perhaps have an easterly wind. We can also suppose otherwise, as follows. In summer a remarkable high-pressure area exists over the Pacific Ocean, which is situated to the east of us. We suppose that the fall of air temperature is small because of the moist air over the ocean, and the air pressure does not fall much upward. In this way we suppose that the high-pressure area continues to exist throughout the air up to the high atmosphere, and causes the easterly wind. This supposition is supported by the fact that the upper easterly wind appears mostly together with the remarkable high pressure over the ocean. However, the idea is only a supposition, and one must collect much material to reach a certain conclusion.

*Sir Napier Shaw: The air and its ways. Plate XVIII.

4. Conclusion.

  1. All observations by pilot balloon are by the two-theodolite method. The total number of observations is 1288.
  2. The daily variation of the speed and the direction of the wind is distinguishable up to 2 km.
  3. The summer and winter monsoons halt at the height of 2 km.
  4. Above 2 km, an almost constant wind blows with the mean direction of 276°.
  5. Above 2 km, an almost always westerly wind blows in spring, autumn and winter, but other winds are not lacking in summer; even in the air above 10 km one often observes an easterly wind.
  6. The wind at the ground is strongest at 14ʰ and weakest at 6ʰ. At the height of 100 m, the speed for 6ʰ equals that for 10ʰ. At a height above 400 m, the wind for 6ʰ is stronger than for 14ʰ.
  7. The speed of the wind increases rapidly from the ground to the height of 300 m. In the layer 500–1500 m the increase is slow.
  8. The wind, both upper and lower, is strong in winter and weak in summer.
  9. In summer the wind strengthens only up to the height of 200 m, and does not increase much further. In winter it becomes very strong in the upper air.
  10. Besides the local wind, the monsoon and the general circulation, there also exists an irregular upper wind. The cause of the last seems to be the high-pressure area that occupies a large area of the world.
