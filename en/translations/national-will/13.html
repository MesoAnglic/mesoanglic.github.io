Chapter 13

MiG-21

Group Captain Sami climbed rapidly to 2,000 metres. The enemy had not appeared from the blinding direction of the sun; it seemed they lacked the nerve for a close fight. Almost at once his RWR screamed: a fire-control radar at his twelve o'clock had locked onto him. None of the remaining Pakistani aircraft in the area carried medium-range missiles. A real problem. He switched on his own fire-control radar and saw, fifteen kilometres out, a formation moving away at ninety degrees. He judged this was not a genuine withdrawal; they were simply trying to open the range and exploit their beyond-visual-range advantage. They would turn back soon enough.

"Griffin One, Black Spider Two: descend immediately. Leave the area. Do not delay."

"Group Captain?"

"That's an order. I'll hold them off."

"Copy. Latest report: No. 5 Squadron is on the way," Abbas said.

The Saab AWACS could not establish a data link with the older-model F-16s or the Thunders; it could only provide limited early warning by voice. This gave the enemy a second advantage. To avoid a pointless tangle, the group captain decided to send the others away. The Indians did not want a close fight; better to let his people go while he bought time alone.

The Pakistan Air Force was professional and brave, but its equipment came from a bewildering variety of sources. Compatibility problems; incompatible interfaces, Magic missiles that would not leave the rail;1 these things plagued the service constantly.

The moment the other aircraft departed, the group captain's radar warning receiver shrieked again. The enemy had turned ninety degrees as predicted. Their radar was tracking him without pause. A second volley of R-27s was coming.

"Black Spider One, enemy has launched missiles. Two rounds, eleven o'clock, nineteen kilometres," the AWACS reported.

Sami pushed the nose down, diving to break the radar track. He knew the MiG-29's look-down capability was severely limited;2 against terrain clutter, the aircraft would quickly lose its ability to maintain a range track, and the R-27, a semi-active missile dependent on continuous radar illumination, would go stupid.

"Enemy formation has split. Some are disengaging," the AWACS reported.

Sami guessed the short-legged MiG-21 Bisons3 were running low on fuel. This interested him.

"Fortress,4 bearing and speed of the departing aircraft?"

"Twenty degrees right. Approximately Mach 0.5. Four-ship formation."

The radar warning receiver had gone quiet. The MiG-29s had apparently lost him. The group captain had no desire to tangle with a MiG-29 formation at twenty kilometres. He shut off his radar, followed the AWACS controller's bearing, and held at very low altitude, chasing the four MiG-21s. These were his primary targets.

"Black Spider lead, missiles have lost lock. I can see you heading toward the border."

"Fortress, are they ahead of me? The MiG-21s?"

"Any other activity?"

"The MiG-29 four-ship has turned north. Forty kilometres from you. They may be heading back to Adampur."

Sami knew the MiG-29 threat had essentially evaporated. He climbed slightly out of the mountains and switched the radar back on, scanning directly ahead. Four closely spaced contacts appeared almost immediately. He locked the aperture onto the trailing target. The contact made no evasive move; evidently these aircraft had very poor radar warning equipment.5

"Black Spider lead, Squadron Leader Hassan is at your four o'clock, fifty kilometres, two-ship."

"Fortress, put the Falcons onto the MiG-29s to the north. Leave this to me." The group captain was all but taking over the entire engagement. He knew perfectly well that Ahmed Hassan was the sort of man who picked the softest target available; that, of course, was something Sami had taught him years ago.

"Black Spider, this is Falcon. We cannot intercept that many MiG-29s." Hassan's voice came over the frequency, aggrieved.

"Fire your missiles. Drive them off. This end is mine."

As he spoke, Sami powered up the PL-56 on his wingtip and waited for the launch cue. For this elderly infrared missile, a dead-astern shot was an opportunity that came once in a lifetime.

The Falcon pair immediately engaged the Tridents'7 MiG-29 formation in a medium-range missile exchange at fifty kilometres. Both sides turned away to evade after firing. At that range, neither was going to hit anything.

At the far end of the battlefield, everything was still quiet. Perhaps they've expended their medium-range missiles, the group captain thought. If so, they're useless now.

"Black Spider, single enemy aircraft at your seven o'clock, slightly above your altitude. SA-6 radar to the east has come active. Initiate electronic countermeasures immediately," someone aboard the AWACS said, his tone as level as if this were a routine exercise.

The group captain did not comply at once. He saw no need. Since climbing, various radar pulses had been sweeping his aircraft without pause; he could not spare the attention for every one of them. There was something more pressing to worry about. He could see one MiG-21 glued to his tail, but the other aircraft was nowhere to be found. That was the real threat: an unseen MiG that could be lurking in any quarter, like a shark beneath murky water.

Notes

1. Compatibility problems: The narrative here broadens a theme introduced in Chapters 4–6 with Sami's malfunctioning Magic missiles. The PAF's inventory draws from French (Mirage, Magic), American (F-16, AIM-120), Chinese (JF-17, PL-5), and Swedish (Saab 2000) sources, each with its own data-link protocols and integration requirements. The inability of the Saab AWACS to link with older F-16As or JF-17s, described in this chapter, is a concrete instance of this problem. ↩

2. MiG-29 look-down limitations: The early-model MiG-29B operated by the IAF was equipped with the N019 Rubin pulse-Doppler radar, which had well-documented difficulties distinguishing low-flying targets from ground clutter, particularly in mountainous terrain. This was a known weakness that Soviet-era doctrine compensated for by relying on ground-controlled interception. Sami's dive exploits this limitation. ↩

3. MiG-21 Bison (米格21野牛): The MiG-21bis upgraded under the IAF's Bison programme with a Kopyo radar, R-77 capability, and improved avionics. Despite the upgrades, the airframe's limited internal fuel capacity gives it a combat radius of roughly 250 km, making fuel state a constant concern in prolonged engagements. ↩

4. Fortress (堡垒): The AWACS callsign. The Saab 2000 Erieye (see C7N4) is now functioning as the Pakistani command-and-control platform, providing Sami with voice-relayed early warning and bearing information in lieu of a data link. ↩

5. Poor radar warning equipment: The MiG-21bis, even in its Bison upgrade, carried the SPO-15 Beryoza RWR, a system of limited sensitivity compared to Western equivalents. Its failure to alert the pilots to Sami's radar lock is consistent with the type's known EW limitations. ↩

6. PL-5 (PL-5导弹): The PL-5E, a Chinese short-range infrared-homing air-to-air missile derived from the Soviet K-13/AA-2 Atoll lineage. Pakistan ordered 900 PL-5Es for its JF-17 fleet. The missile has all-aspect capability, a maximum range of approximately 14 km, and a 40g manoeuvre limit. The "elderly" characterisation in the text is the author's; the PL-5E was a 1990s upgrade of a 1960s design. ↩

7. Tridents (三叉戟中队): IAF No. 223 Squadron "Tridents," a MiG-29 unit. The squadron was originally equipped with MiG-23s and converted to MiG-29s in 1990, based at Adampur (see C11N8). The Tridents' engagement with Hassan's Falcons at fifty kilometres, with both sides firing and evading, illustrates the futility of BVR exchanges when neither side can maintain a track long enough for the missiles to guide. ↩

萨米上校迅速爬升到2000米，敌机没有出现在耀眼的太阳方向，看上去他们没有勇气近战，很快上校就被12点钟方向的火控雷达咬住了，这里仅剩的几架巴基斯坦飞机都没有中距弹，真的是一个大麻烦。上校打开火控雷达，在屏幕上看到15公里外，一个机群正在以夹角90度飞离，他判断这不是真的撤离，他们只是想保持距离，发挥远距离攻击的优势而已，很快它们就会重新把机头对准这里。

"格里芬一号，毒蜘蛛二号，你们立即降低高度，离开战场，不要拖延。"

"上校？"

"这是命令，我来拖延他们。"

"明白，刚得到的消息，第5中队正在赶来。"阿巴斯说道。

萨伯预警机无法与这里的F-16早期型或者枭龙进行数据链连接，只能提供有限的早期预警，这让远处的敌人占到了第二个便宜，为了避免不必要的纠缠，上校决定让其他飞机离开，反正敌人不准备近战，不如让他们离开，由他自己来周旋。

巴基斯坦有一支专业而又英勇的空军，但是他的装备来源也相当的繁杂，诸如接口不匹配或者魔术导弹无法离开发射架这一类兼容问题总是不断地缠绕着这支军队。

其余飞机刚刚飞离，上校的雷达告警器就尖叫起来，敌机果然转了90度，雷达又一次开始对他进行不间断的跟踪，这说明第二轮的R-27攻击就要来了。

电台里预警机的声音："黑蜘蛛一号，敌机发射了导弹，2枚，11点方向19公里。"

上校压低机头，以一个俯冲规避雷达跟踪，他很清楚米格29的雷达下视能力相当的有限，在地形遮蔽下，它们很快会失去测距跟踪能力，而R-27导弹是需要依靠雷达照射的半主动导弹。

预警机："敌机群分散了，有一些在脱离。"

萨米上校猜测很可能是短腿的米格21野牛战斗机的油料不够了，这引起了他的兴趣。

"堡垒，离开的敌机机群的方位角，速度？"

"右侧20度，速度约0.5马赫，4机编队。"

雷达告警器没有再响起，看起来米格29迷糊了，上校不想和20公里外的米格29机群纠缠；他关闭了雷达，按照与预警机的指引，保持很低的高度向4架米格21追去，这才是他的首要打击目标。

"黑蜘蛛长机，导弹已经失的了，我看到你在向敌方过境飞去。"

"堡垒，他们在我前面吗？那些米格21？"

"还有什么其余的情况？"

"米格29的4机编队已经掉头向北，离你40公里，可能会转弯飞回亚当布尔。"

上校知道米格29的威胁已经基本解除，就稍稍从群山中升高了一点儿度，重新打开雷达搜索正前方，很快发现了四个靠的很近的目标，他以雷达孔径方式，抓住了最后一个目标，对手一直没有躲闪，可见这些飞机的雷达告警装置相当的落后。

"黑蜘蛛长机，哈桑中队长双机在4点钟方向50公里外。"

"堡垒，你让猎鹰对付北面的米格29，这里交给我。"上校几乎想接替整个指挥，他很清楚，艾哈迈德哈桑，是一个捡软柿子捏的行家，当然这一点是很久以前自己教他的。

"毒蜘蛛，我是猎鹰，我们无法拦截如此多的米格29。"哈桑的愤愤地声音出现在了频道里。

"发射你的导弹，把他们赶走。这里交给我。"

萨米说着，接通了翼尖上的PL-5导弹弹头，静待允许发射的许可出现，对于这种老式的红外制导导弹来说，这是一个千载难逢的6点钟机会。

猎鹰中队双机，立刻展开了与印度三叉戟中队的米格29机群，相隔50公里的中程导弹对射，双方在射击后都立即转向规避，很显然这种打法谁也打不中谁。

在战场遥远的另一端，一切还都很安静。也可能他们的中距弹打完了，如果这样，根本就指望不上他们了，上校想。

"黑蜘蛛，敌机单机在你后方7点略高于你的位置；探测到东面萨姆6阵地雷达开机，立即进行电子压制。"预警机里有人说话，语调如同平常演习一样平缓。

上校没有立即照办，他觉得毫无必要，升高高度后，各种无线电脉冲正在不间断地扫描他的飞机，他没功夫去关心这些破事；现在有更要紧的事情要注意，他只能看到一架米格21紧咬在后面，而另一架飞机不知道在哪儿？这才是最大的威胁，因为这架米格可能躲在自己的各个方向，这才是最大的麻烦，就象一条藏在混浊的海面下的鲨鱼。
