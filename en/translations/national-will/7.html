Chapter 7

Counterattack

Flying Officer Goyal walked outside the squadron headquarters. It was late in the evening. On the runway, several Su-30MKIs were starting their engines; he could identify them as No. 102 Squadron1 aircraft. Goyal sparred frequently with 102 Squadron's pilots in the officers' mess and always came out on top. No. 102 had been nothing but a reconnaissance unit; around 2000, when the last MiG-25s reached the end of their airframe lives, the squadron had even been disbanded for a time. Its people always stood a head shorter before No. 24 Squadron. Even after re-equipping with Su-30s, they remained second-string within 13 Wing. A few years earlier, a detachment from 102 Squadron had been posted to Chabua Air Force Station2 in Assam, a godforsaken forward base with primitive facilities, reportedly only seventy-five kilometres from the Chinese border. Every pilot still at Bareilly lived in dread of being packed off to that desolate backwater.

"They've got a mission?" Goyal grabbed a passing technical NCO.

"That's right. Covering No. 7 Wing's Jaguars.3 New contact report; they've located a concealed Pakistani flak battery. The very one you found, by the way." The NCO said it with a grin, which embarrassed Goyal somewhat. He remembered how close those guns had come to bringing him down.

"Flying Officer, we all heard about your score this afternoon. Two enemy aircraft in one go; everyone says you're getting the Vir Chakra. The boys in 102 are all raring to go now, saying our military-technological edge over Pakistan is ten years. Everyone wants to bag more Pakistani aircraft. Maybe that'll spare them a posting to Assam." The NCO finished with a broad smile.

"I'd rather everyone didn't take this too lightly." The flying officer spoke without a trace of self-interest. The thought of the Pakistani pilot he had fought six hours earlier still sent a chill through him.

"Doubt there'll be any air fighting at all. They're all scared witless; probably won't dare take off." The NCO shook Goyal's hand firmly and left.

I have to warn them. They cannot underestimate the enemy. Goyal thought this as he watched two aircraft taxi forward one behind the other, then leave the runway. These two Su-30s were to rendezvous over Chandigarh with No. 7 Wing's MiG-21s and Jaguar strike aircraft and eliminate the flak battery lurking on the forward edge. They may have made a mistake, Goyal reflected. At night, most aircraft were incapable of radar-evading low-altitude flight. A formation this large would be easily detected by Pakistani radar.

The Pakistan Air Force was not asleep. A Saab 20004 from No. 13 Squadron, orbiting 250 kilometres from the border, detected a small group of Indian aircraft moving north. The information was passed to ground headquarters, and the formidable No. 5 Squadron's5 F-16s scrambled to intercept.

Squadron Leader6 Ahmed Hassan lifted his F-16 off the runway at Shorkot7 and surged toward the bearing the AWACS had given him. He and his wingman held at roughly 4,000 metres, slightly above the Indian formation's altitude. The Saab 2000's data link, which had been tracking the targets continuously, was feeding Indian air contacts onto his vertical situation display; they were still beyond the search range of his own radar.

"Rashid, listen. One salvo. Attack only the two slower targets trailing at the rear." The squadron leader spoke to his wingman while they were still outside missile range; a brief moment to coordinate.

"Copy!" On their vertical displays, a cluster of contacts was moving: a group of MiG-21s escorting two Jaguars heading north. For now they were hugging the border, but they could cross at any moment.

"This afternoon they nearly did old Sami in. A Su-30 from a deep rear base ambushed him. Shows their tactics have improved. Anyway, I'd say Sami's days are over."

"If the group captain had had the sense to leave the combat units, he'd be an air commodore8 by now. You know, having a general officer in the air causes all sorts of headaches for the chain of command. Maybe he really is past it. But that miraculous strike on the Afghan border9 when he was young — you have to envy that."

"Was he with 14 Squadron then?" Rashid asked.

"No, No. 9 Squadron, I think."

The voice of the distant AWACS broke in over the radio: "Falcon leader, eleven o'clock, ninety kilometres. Two Su-30MKIs closing."

Squadron Leader Hassan adjusted the magnification on his vertical display and saw the two Su-30s moving along the Indian side of the formation, performing some manner of half-hearted escort.

"They always keep these precious babies tucked away safe and sound," Hassan said with a laugh.

"Should we change the attack sequence? We might get a shot at them," the wingman asked.

"No. Don't engage supermanoeuvrable targets at long range. Besides, they haven't crossed the border. Continue tracking the Jaguar ISs."

After several minutes of tracking, the F-16 pair, approaching the speed of sound, had closed rapidly on the trailing Jaguars.

"Squadron Leader, they've crossed the border. They don't appear to have detected us."

"With those MiG-21s for escorts?" the squadron leader said contemptuously.

The two Jaguars at the rear of the formation had entered the F-16s' forward hemisphere at thirty-six kilometres: the optimal engagement range for the AIM-120C.10 Once launched, the missiles could almost immediately acquire the target on their own active radar.

"Radar on. Track-while-scan." The squadron leader gave the order.

"Falcon requests weapons free," Hassan said into the radio.

After a pause, the AWACS cleared them to fire. Hassan pressed the launch button.

"Fox Three, launch."

"Away."

Two missiles ignited and left the leader's pylons in quick succession; the wingman launched two more an instant later. The missile-radar activation countdown appeared on the HUD. Seconds later the squadron leader watched the missile radars go active and smoothly acquire their targets. At the same moment, the two Jaguars began a panicked descent; they had evidently realised they were under attack. Far to the northeast, the two Su-30s turned and began accelerating toward the engagement.

"Squadron Leader, their Su-30s have found us. Seventy-five kilometres, still accelerating."

"Ignore them. We're withdrawing." Hassan executed a descending spiral and turned west.

"I'd love to see the results," Rashid said.

"Let the AWACS handle that."

The AWACS came over the radio: "Falcon flight, enemy has launched R-77 missiles.11 Five o'clock, sixty-five kilometres. Estimated time of arrival, two minutes."

"Are they joking? They think they can hit us from there?" Hassan lit the afterburners and accelerated away. He knew perfectly well that such an out-of-envelope shot was utterly meaningless.

"Falcon flight, Target One hit. It's gone down into the Hajira valley."12

"Got him?"

"That's how it looks."

"Falcon flight, Target Two has also disappeared. It's going down." The air surveillance operator aboard the AWACS.

"Excellent. My tally's just passed the group captain's, I think. If you don't count the drones."

"Heh, you'll overtake him for certain, Squadron Leader," Rashid said.

"Pity, though. The group captain's kill from twenty-four years ago can never be matched."

The two F-16s headed directly for Shorkot at Mach 1. The pair of R-77s chased them for a while, then burned out their fuel and fell into the desert.

Notes

1. No. 102 Squadron "Trisonics" (第102中队): One of the IAF's most storied units. Originally raised as 102 Survey Flight in 1951, the squadron was reformed in 1981 on the MiG-25RBK/RU Foxbat for strategic reconnaissance. The nickname derives from the MiG-25's Mach 3 capability. In May 1997, a 102 Squadron MiG-25 famously broke the sound barrier over Islamabad, triggering a fruitless PAF F-16 scramble. The squadron was "number-plated" (disbanded) in 2003 when the MiG-25 fleet was retired, and reformed on the Su-30MKI around 2011. In the novel's timeline the reconstitution has already taken place, though the squadron retains the institutional insecurity of a recently revived unit. ↩

2. Chabua Air Force Station (贾布瓦空军基地): An IAF station in Dibrugarh district, Assam, under Eastern Air Command. No. 102 Squadron is indeed based at Chabua in reality, operating Su-30MKIs as part of 14 Wing. The novel's claim of seventy-five kilometres from the Chinese border is broadly consistent; it is among the IAF's most forward-deployed Su-30MKI stations. The author's characterisation of it as a desolate posting reflects its remote location in India's northeast, far from the social and professional infrastructure of bases like Bareilly or Pune. ↩

3. No. 7 Wing's Jaguars (第7联队的美洲虎): No. 7 Wing at Ambala Air Force Station comprises squadrons operating the SEPECAT Jaguar in the ground-attack role. The Jaguar, known in IAF service as the Shamsher ("Sword of Justice"), has served since 1979 and remains the IAF's dedicated strike platform. IAF Jaguars are also a critical component of India's airborne nuclear deterrent, a detail that becomes significant in the following chapter. ↩

4. Saab 2000 AEW&C (萨伯2000): A twin-turboprop aircraft fitted with the Erieye active electronically scanned array radar in a dorsal fairing, capable of detecting airborne targets at up to 450 km. Pakistan received its first Saab 2000 Erieye in December 2009, inducted into No. 13 Squadron at PAF Base Minhas (Kamra). The novel's assignment of the aircraft to this squadron is historically accurate, though the unit was later redesignated and additional AEW&C squadrons were formed. ↩

5. No. 5 Squadron "Falcons" (第5中队): See C1N10. One of the PAF's premier combat units, operating F-16C/D Block 52+ fighters. The squadron has a long operational history spanning all three Indo-Pakistani wars. Its scramble to intercept an Indian strike package is consistent with its historical role as a quick-reaction alert unit. ↩

6. Squadron Leader Ahmed Hassan (艾哈迈德·哈桑少校): A fictional PAF F-16 pilot with No. 5 Squadron. The original Chinese uses 少校 (Major) and at one point 少尉 (Pilot Officer); the latter is clearly an authorial error. "Major" has been rendered as "Squadron Leader" throughout, following PAF convention: the PAF uses RAF-style ranks, and the equivalent of army Major is Squadron Leader. This is consistent with the translation's practice of using service-specific ranks (Group Captain for Sami, Flying Officer for Goyal, Wing Commander for Mohsin). ↩

7. Shorkot (肖尔科特): The Chinese 肖尔科特 (Xiāo'ěrkètè) most plausibly corresponds to Shorkot, a city in Punjab near PAF Base Rafiqui, which has hosted F-16 operations. The phonetics could also yield Sialkot, closer to the Indian border, but Sialkot is not traditionally a PAF fighter base. Rafiqui's proximity to Shorkot and its documented role as an F-16 operating location make this the more likely identification, though certainty is impossible from the Chinese transliteration alone. ↩

8. Air Commodore (少将): The Chinese 少将 literally means "Major General," but in PAF rank equivalence the one-star rank is Air Commodore. A group captain (equivalent to Colonel) promoted out of combat units would reach Air Commodore as his next rank. The translation uses the PAF-appropriate term. Hassan's remark that "having a general officer in the air causes headaches" reflects the real organisational tension of very senior officers insisting on flying combat sorties. ↩

9. Afghan border engagements: Between 1986 and 1988, PAF F-16s shot down at least eight Soviet and Afghan aircraft (Su-22s, MiG-23s, an An-26, and an Su-25) that strayed into Pakistani airspace during the Soviet-Afghan War. Hassan's reference to Sami's "miraculous strike on the Afghan border" may refer to a ground-attack mission rather than an air-to-air kill, since Sami's career is associated with Mirage and JF-17 aircraft rather than F-16s. The exact nature of the exploit is left deliberately vague. ↩

10. AIM-120C (AIM-120C型导弹): The AIM-120C Advanced Medium-Range Air-to-Air Missile (AMRAAM), the primary BVR weapon of the PAF's F-16 Block 52+ fleet. An active radar-homing missile that guides itself in the terminal phase without requiring the launching aircraft to maintain radar illumination. The thirty-six-kilometre engagement described represents a close shot well within the no-escape zone against a non-manoeuvring target; the missile's active seeker would acquire almost immediately after launch, as the text states. ↩

11. R-77 (R-77导弹): The Vympel R-77 (NATO reporting name AA-12 Adder), a medium-range active radar-homing air-to-air missile and the Indian Su-30MKI's primary BVR weapon. At sixty-five kilometres against retreating F-16s in afterburner, the shot was fired well beyond effective range; the missile would exhaust its propellant before reaching the target, as the narrative confirms. The Su-30 crews' decision to fire regardless suggests either desperation or a doctrinal preference for forcing the enemy to react. ↩

12. Hajira valley (哈吉拉山谷): Hajira is a sector along the LoC in Pakistani-administered Kashmir, west of the Haji Pir Pass. The terrain is rugged and mountainous, consistent with the description of an aircraft "disappearing" into it. The area saw significant fighting during the 1965 war and has remained a sensitive sector along the LoC. ↩

戈亚中尉走到中队指挥部外面，天色已经很晚了，他看到跑道上几架苏30MKI在正在启动，他能认出这是第102中队的飞机。戈亚经常和102中队的飞行员们在军人食堂抬杠，每次都能占到上风。102中队以往只是一支侦察机部队，2000年前后当最后一批米格25的机体寿命终结后，还曾一度解散过，所以他们的人在24中队面前总是矮半截，即使换装了苏30，但是在13联队内部，他们仍然屈居二线，几年前102中队的一个分队还被调派到阿萨姆邦的贾布瓦空军基地，那是一个设施简陋、鸟不拉屎的前线基地，据说离中国边境只有75公里，仍然留在巴雷利的102中队飞的行员人人自危，生怕被打发到那个寂寞偏僻的地区……

"他们有任务？"戈亚拉住一旁的一名技术士官问道。

"没错，掩护第7联队的美洲虎，新的敌情通报，发现了巴基斯坦的一个隐蔽的高射炮阵地，对了，就是你发现的那个高射炮阵地嘛。"士官笑着说道，这让戈亚有一些难为情，他想起了自己差点被那个阵地的高射炮给打下来。

"中尉，你下午的战绩我们都知道了，一次干掉了2架敌机，都说你要得勇士轮形勋章了。现在102中队的家伙们都跃跃欲试，都说我们的军事技术优势超越巴基斯坦10年，大家都想干掉更多的巴基斯坦飞机，也许这样就不必到荒凉的萨姆邦去了。"军士咧嘴笑着说道。

"我希望大家还是不要太掉以轻心的好。"中尉说道，他的话完全没有半点私心，想起6个小时前与之交手的巴基斯坦飞行员，他就觉得浑身有一丝寒意。

"怕是根本不会有空战了，他们都吓破胆，大概不敢升空了。"军士说完，使劲和戈亚握了握手，转身离开了。

"必须得提醒他们，不能太轻敌"戈亚心里想着，看着两架飞机一前一后向前滑行，然后离开了跑道。这两架飞机将要在昌迪尔加上空与第七联队的米格21和美洲虎攻击机会和，去拔掉那个埋伏在前沿的高射炮阵地。戈亚上尉心想，他们也许犯下了一个错误，在夜间，大部分飞机都无法进行躲避雷达的低空飞行，这么大的机群很容易被巴基斯坦的雷达发现。

巴基斯坦空军没有睡着，13中队的一架萨伯2000在距离边界250公里的地方，发现了一小群印军的飞机正向北移动，他将消息递到地面指挥部，随即最强悍的第5中队F-16战斗机起飞反击。

艾哈迈德哈桑少校驾驶他的F-16战斗机离开了肖尔科特基地的跑道，向着预警机指引的航向直扑过来。他与僚机的高度保持在4000米左右，这个高度略高于印度空军编队的高度。一直在监视目标的萨伯2000的数据链正将印度一方的空中目标数据传输到他的垂直显示器上，这些目标还在他的机载雷达搜索范围之外。

"拉希德，听着，一次齐射，只攻击落在最后的两个较低速的目标。"少尉对他的僚机说道，趁着现在还没有到导弹的射程之内，他们稍微沟通了一下。

"明白！"僚机回应道。他们的垂直方向显示器上，一簇光电正在移动，这是一群米格21簇拥着两架美洲虎在向北方移动，目前他们还紧贴这边界，但是随时可能会超越边界。

"今天下午，他们差点把老萨米给干掉了，一架从纵深机场起飞的苏30偷袭了老家伙，这说明他们的战术有一些进步。总之，我认为萨米上校的时代已经终结了。"

"如果上校知趣退出战斗部队的话，早就是少将了。你知道，一个少将在空中，会给指挥带来很多麻烦。他也许真的老了。不过，他年轻的时候，在阿富汗边境那次神奇的攻击真是招人嫉妒。"

"那时候他在14中队？"拉希德问。

"不，我记得是第9中队。"

电台里远处预警机传来的声音打断了飞行员的谈话："猎鹰队长，11点钟方向90公里，2架苏30MKI正在接近。"

哈桑少校调节垂直显示器倍率，在屏幕上看到了这两架苏30，正在机群靠印度一侧移动，显然是在做某种不伦不类的掩护。

"他们总是把这些宝贝疙瘩藏得严严实实的。"哈桑笑着说道。

"是不是要修改攻击次序，我们可能打到他们。"僚机问道……

"不，不要攻击远距离的超机动目标。何况他们没有越过边界，还是继续监视美洲虎IS。"

几分钟的跟踪后，接近音速的F-16的双机编队已经越来越接近落在最后的美洲虎了。

"少校，他们已经跨过边界了，看上去一直没有发现我们。"

"就凭那些米格21？"少校轻蔑地说道。

掉在编队后面的两架美洲虎已经进入了F-16编队前方36公里距离，这是AIM-120C型导弹最佳的攻击位置，导弹发射后，几乎可以立刻靠自身的主动雷达找到目标。

"打开雷达，进入扫描跟踪。"少校命令道。

"猎鹰请求开火。"哈桑对着电台说道。

等了一会儿，预警机允许他们开火。哈桑上校按下了发射按钮。

"福克斯3，发射。"

"已经发射了。"

两枚导弹先后从长机的挂架下点火飞离，紧接着僚机也发射了两枚导弹。HUD上的雷达开机倒计时随即开始，几秒钟后，少校看着导弹雷达开机，然后顺利捕获到了目标。与此同时，两架美洲虎开始惊慌失措地降低高度，他们显然已经知道被攻击了。遥远东北方2架苏30开始转向，然后加速向这边扑过来。

"少校，他们的苏30发现我们了，距离75公里，还在加速飞行中。"

"别管他们，我们撤退。"哈桑说着做了一个向下盘旋的动作，转向向西。

"真想看看攻击结果。"拉希德说道。

"留给预警机看吧。"

预警机传来的声音："猎鹰小队，敌机发射了R-77导弹，5点钟方位，65公里外，到达时间预计2分钟。"

"开玩笑，他们以为这样能打中我们？"哈桑打开加力加速脱离，他很清楚，对方这样不计射程的攻击毫无意义。

"猎鹰小队，1号目标被击中了，消失在了哈吉拉山谷里。"预警机传来了第一个战果。

"打中了？"

"应该是这样。"

"猎鹰小队，2号目标也消失了，它正在坠毁。"预警机里的空中监视人员接着说道。

"太好了，我的战果好像已经超越萨米上校了，如果不计算无人机的话。"

"呵呵，少校，你一定可以超越他。"拉希德说道。

"可惜，萨米上校24年前的那个战果是不可替代的。"拉希德说道。

两架F-16以1马赫的速度直接向着肖尔科特的基地飞去，尾随的两枚R-77追击了一段时间，耗尽了燃料就坠毁在了沙漠里。
