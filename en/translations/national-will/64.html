Chapter 64

Hit Rate

Kushan knew a missile was somewhere close, but when the pilot in the rear seat looked back, he could not for the moment place where it had gone. All he could see was a single white trail spiralling up from below. Perhaps the missile had already lost the target? the pilot thought. The Su-30 was not an aircraft that could never be shot down; but he hoped that, whatever happened, it would not be today.

The missile detonated in an instant, violently, in the observation blind spot behind and below the aircraft, and the huge fighter began to roll hard. Lieutenant Kushan did not need to look back to know the aircraft was finished. The savage tumbling began to drag his consciousness away. He watched the airspeed reading fall to a low figure before he hauled the canopy-jettison handle, and the zero-zero ejection seats1 flung both crew clear of the doomed machine. Turning over in the air, waiting for his chute to open, the lieutenant saw his own falling fighter blown to pieces; the missile had plainly detonated very close beneath the aircraft.

"Command, we have lost a Lion. The pilots appear to have ejected."

The AWACS reported to the ground command post.

The Chief of Air Staff had inserted himself stubbornly into the operation, and in doing so had disrupted the existing command structure. For the moment everyone, the AWACS included, had to obey him, and yet he was the man furthest from the battlefield. For several years the Indian Air Force had been experimenting with a new command arrangement, one that gave airborne command authority priority over ground command, a method the Chief of Staff himself had pushed hard. But an arrangement is one thing; the moment his orders came over the radio into the air, everything changed.2

"I saw it. Bring the Torch flight all the way home," Bifrata answered. He had a premonition this would not be the only Su-30 lost today.

The order to withdraw came, perhaps, a little late. The Torch flight was already sinking into confusion. On the ground the Pakistani fire-control radars were tracking the flight's four aircraft without a break, and those radars were plainly very close: the flight was at a range where they could absolutely burn through the jamming.

The distant Flying Snake flight (the electronic-warfare aircraft) was 100 kilometres off, accelerating toward the battle area, but by now it too could do nothing. The pre-battle electronic reconnaissance had been carried out by the drone units, and they had indeed gathered some emission data from the Pakistani side's radars; the Flying Snake flight had stored those signatures as well. But the radars the Pakistanis were using today had signatures that were an entire blank in the records. These radars were known to run short, shifting pulses across rapidly varying frequency bands, and follower deception3 (recording the incoming emission, delaying it, and transmitting it back to confuse the enemy radar) was almost wholly ineffective. This meant the enemy on the ground certainly had strong technical support directing the Pakistani operators (the only conclusion the Indian pilots could draw from years of dealing with Pakistan's ground-to-air units). The missiles scattered through the mountains began to fire, one after another, and dazzling white light filled the sky.

The FD-2000 system was capable of engaging multiple targets at once, and in theory could fire on all nine remaining aircraft; but today the ground commander did not appear greedy. He aimed at only four targets, and put two missiles onto each.

As Flight Lieutenant Sahili4 was scrambling to direct the Torch flight clear of the missile attack, his rear-seat weapons officer made a startling discovery on the data-link display: the Thunder flight fleeing to the west had actually begun to turn. The flight lieutenant realised he had walked into a shameless piece of bait-fishing.

Within twenty seconds, two of the Su-30s in the formation were struck in succession and went down in the Dumaili5 hill country. The lieutenant saw all four aircrew take to their chutes (Kushan's aircraft could not see it), but he knew this was a long way from the border and rescue was out of the question. Given that they had just taken part in the dam-bombing operation, how Pakistan would treat these No. 31 Squadron pilots was a thing very much worth worrying about.

This engagement was the FD-2000's first use in combat, and its hit rate did not seem especially high (ten rounds bringing down three aircraft). Clearly the figure was somewhat below the hit rates written in the export brochures propped beside the display stand. Even so, the result was already enough to have Air Commodore Islam6, inside the Saab 2000, grinning ear to ear. The air commodore now had no mind at all for the rest of the battle. He had just detailed the AWACS communications NCO to contact the ground troops and immediately search for the ejected Indian pilots and the wreckage, as though terrified the wreckage might slip away. His thoughts had long since flown off the battlefield; he was thinking that he ought to bring every foreign journalist out to the wreckage to take photographs, so they could see how an aircraft India produced at a rate of a bare twelve per year had, in a single morning, become three heaps of scrap.

Air Commodore Islam had reckoned a touch conservatively. His ace card was at that moment leading a four-ship flight, hard on the tail of the seven Su-30s falling back. Perhaps Sami's ambition ran larger still: what he hoped to destroy in one stroke was half a year of Indian production.

"Group Captain Sami, the enemy aircraft are fleeing southeast. No other aircraft observed taking off from Indian-side airfields. That electronic-warfare aircraft has stayed over the Sialkot7 area. It is jamming communications." The controller aboard the AWACS.

"I've already seen it," Sami answered. At the same moment his half-fuel warning tone chimed once. Sami switched the reminder off, entirely unconcerned.

"Abbas, you're too slow. Don't come chasing over here. Do your best to drive off that damned jammer by the border; while it sits there, we can't see the Indian fighters taking off and landing west of Adampur," Sami said, watching his multifunction display. Abbas was too far from the battle to have cut off the enemy, which disappointed him somewhat.

"Understood."

On the screen, the distant Two flight began to turn toward the lone electronic-reconnaissance aircraft.

Ever since data-link picture-format information support had arrived, communication between pilots seemed to have become terse. For an old hand like Sami, this was without doubt a technology that raised the efficiency of command.

"Thunder squadron leader, your aircraft is past half fuel. Please take note," the AWACS reminded him again.

"Mm, I know," Sami answered, brushing it off.

"Listen, we're going to make a maximum-effort push (a strike that disregards the fuel needed for the return leg). You may leave the battle at your own discretion at any time, or put down nearby at Allama Iqbal International Airport,"8 Sami said to his pilots over the VHF; his words, of course, would certainly be heard by Air Commodore Islam aboard the AWACS.

"You cannot land at an international airport. This... cannot be permitted," the air commodore cut in at once over the radio, hurrying to call a halt. He could not tolerate Sami rewriting the rules on his own say-so.

"Listen, old friend, my radar has already seen their leader. He's fifteen kilometres ahead. I've taken up a very good position; maybe I can bring him down near the border."

"No. Whatever happens, you cannot land at an international airport."

"Maybe we can take an Indian fighter squadron leader alive. You could interrogate him yourself. Think of it: Singh would weep."

Sami's oblique needling seemed to work on the air commodore. He fell silent for a while, and did not explicitly forbid the reckless plan.

Notes

1. Zero-zero ejection seat (零零弹射坐椅): A seat certified to fire safely at zero altitude and zero airspeed, i.e. from a stationary aircraft on the ground. The Su-30MKI uses the NPP Zvezda K-36 series, one of the more capable escape systems of its generation. Kushan's crew eject at low altitude and low speed, precisely the envelope the "zero-zero" rating exists for. ↩

2. Airborne versus ground command: The friction that decides this engagement. Bifrata, the Chief of Air Staff, is running the operation by radio from his command post at Jaipur rather than from an aircraft, which leaves him the participant furthest from the fight and slowest to react. Compare the deal Sami extracted from Islam in C60N3, that Islam would command from an AWACS in the air. The novel keeps drawing the same contrast: command-post latency is fatal in this kind of trap. ↩

3. Follower / repeater deception (随动的欺骗): A jamming technique in which the aircraft captures a hostile radar pulse, holds it a controlled interval, then retransmits it to generate false range or angle data in the enemy set. It works only against radars whose pulse and frequency parameters the jammer has already characterised. The FD-2000's engagement radar uses frequency agility and jittered pulse-repetition intervals precisely to defeat this; with no stored signature for the Pakistani emitters, the Flying Snake's deception mode is inert. ↩

4. Sahili's rank drift: The source calls him 上尉 (Flight Lieutenant) here, having used the functional title "deputy squadron leader" in C62. The two are not in conflict: 上尉 is his substantive rank, deputy squadron leader his appointment. This translation keeps Flight Lieutenant for the rank and reserves "deputy squadron leader" for the role. ↩

5. Dumaili (杜梅立): The hill country where the two Su-30s come down, in the FD-2000 ambush arc west of Kharian. The Chinese 杜梅立 (Dùméilì) does not correspond to a readily locatable place; like Jegwala (C63N5), it appears to be one of the author's coined micro-place names for the Kharian foothills. Transliterated as written; referent uncertain. ↩

6. Air Commodore Islam (伊斯拉姆): The source calls him 少将 (major general) throughout this chapter, but Islam is an air officer and holds the PAF one-star rank of Air Commodore, established at his introduction (C34N3). The army-style "major general" is the author's recurring rank-generic slip; this translation keeps Air Commodore throughout and flags the slip once. ↩

7. Sialkot (希亚尔科特): A city in northeastern Punjab, Pakistan, hard against the Working Boundary with Indian-administered Jammu and roughly opposite the Adampur–Amritsar sector. Its position lets the Indian jammer loiter on the Indian side while blanketing the airspace over the Pakistani forward bases. First appearance in the novel. ↩

8. Allama Iqbal International Airport (阿拉玛伊克巴勒国际机场): Lahore's civil airport, the same field Pandit's flight was tasked to strike on the first night of the war (see C19). Sami's readiness to divert a combat sortie onto a busy international runway is the same instinct that put him down almost on the UN envoy's tail at Islamabad in C14; Islam's alarm is the standing objection nobody has ever made stick. ↩

库山知道有一颗导弹就在附近，但是后坐的飞行员朝后看时，一时找不到导弹的去向，他只能看到一条从下方盘旋上升的白色尾迹。也许导弹已经失的了？飞行员想。苏30不是一架无法击落的战斗机，但是，他希望无论如何，不会是今天。

导弹在飞机侧后的观察死角内瞬间猛烈地爆炸，这使得巨大的战斗机开始剧烈地横滚起来。不用回头察看后方，库山中尉知道飞机肯定是不行了。剧烈的翻滚动作使得他的意识开始慢慢消失。他看着空速数字下降到了一个低的位置，才拉下舱罩抛离杆，随即零零弹射坐椅将2名飞行员弹出失控的飞机。中尉在空中翻腾着等待开伞的当口，看到了正在坠落的自己的战斗机被炸得七零八落，可见导弹是在飞机下方非常近的地方才引爆的。

"总部，我们损失了一架狮子。飞行员似乎跳伞了。"

预警机报告地面指挥所道。

空军参谋长固执地参与到行动当中，客观上破坏了原有的指挥体制。眼下所有的人，包括预警机都必须服从他的指挥，但是他本人又是离开战场最远的一个。印度空军最近几年一直在尝试一种新的指挥体制，尝试着使空中指挥权优先于地面指挥权，这也是参谋长本人极力推动的一种方式，但是体制归体制，当他的命令通过无线电出现在空中时，一切又都不同了。

"我看到了，你让火炬小队全部回来。"比夫拉塔回答道，他预感到这不会是今天损失的唯一一架苏30战斗机。

撤退的命令也许来得有些晚了，空中的火炬小队正陷入在一片混乱当中。地面上的巴基斯坦火控雷达正在不间断地跟踪小队中的四架飞机，这些雷达显然离得非常的近，小队正处于这些雷达能够绝对烧穿干扰的距离上。

遥远的飞蛇小队（电子干扰机）正在100公里外，加速赶向战区，不过现在它也已经无能为力了。这次战斗前的电子侦察由无人机部队执行，它们确实收集到了一些巴基斯坦侧的雷达开机信息，飞蛇小队也将这些信号特征加以储存，但是今天巴方使用的雷达，其信号特征则完全处于记录空白中。已知这些雷达的脉冲短促多变，频段变化很快，随动的欺骗（将当前无线电波记录，延时发回，可以给敌方雷达造成信息混乱）几乎处于完全无效状态。这说明敌人在地面上绝对有强有力的技术支援在指点巴方人员（按照印度飞行员与巴基斯坦地空部队多年打交道的经验得出的唯一结论。）分散在山区各处的导弹开始一一开火，眩白色的光芒布满空中。

FD-2000系统具有同时攻击多个目标的能力，理论上可以向剩余的所有9架飞机开火，不过今天的地面指挥员似乎并不贪心，他只瞄准了4个目标，并且向每个目标发射了2枚导弹。

萨希利上尉慌乱地指挥火炬小队躲避导弹攻击时，后坐武器操作员惊奇地从数据链显示中发现，西面逃窜中的雷电战斗机小队竟然开始转弯了。上尉意识到这是一次无耻的钓鱼行动。

20秒内，编队中有2架苏30先后被导弹击中，掉在了杜梅立山区地带，中尉看到了4名飞行员都跳了伞（库山座机没能看到），但是他知道这里离边境很远，救援是不可能的，鉴于他们刚刚参与了水坝轰炸行动，巴基斯坦会怎么对待这些31中队的飞行员，是一件非常的值得担心的事。

这次攻击是FD-2000导弹第一次投入实战，它的命中率似乎并不是很高（10发命中3架），很明显，这个数字比起摆在展台旁的那些外贸宣传手册上写的命中率要低一些。当然，这个结果也已经足够让萨伯2000内的伊斯拉姆少将乐得合不拢嘴了。少将此时完全无心接下来的指挥了，他刚刚指定了预警机上的通讯士官联系地面部队，立即搜索跳伞的印度飞行员和残骸，生怕这些残骸会溜走一样。少将的思绪早就飞出了战场，他想着应该把所有的外国记者都带到残骸边上去拍照，让他们看看这种印度年产量只有区区12架的飞机，在一个上午就变成了3堆废铁。

伊斯拉姆少将的算盘打得稍微有些保守了，他的第一王牌此时正指挥着一个4机小队，紧追回撤中的7架苏30。或许，萨米的野心更大一些，他希望一次干掉的，是印度半年的产量。

"萨米上校，敌机正在往东南逃走。没有发现印度侧的机场有其它飞机起飞。那架电子战飞机停留在了希亚尔科特地区。正在对通讯进行干扰。"预警机内的引导员说道。

"我已经看到了。"萨米回答道。与此同时，他的油量过半提示音响了一次。萨米很无所谓地将这个提醒音频关掉了。

"阿巴斯，你的动作太慢，不要往这里赶了，尽量把边境旁的那架该死的干扰机赶走，它留在哪儿，我们看不清亚当布尔以西的印度战斗机起降。"萨米看这多功能显示器说道，阿巴斯离战场太远，没能拦截住敌人，这让他有些失望。

"明白。"

屏幕上，遥远的第2小队开始转向孤独的电子侦察机。

自从有了数据链图象式的信息支援，飞行员间的联络似乎变得简洁起来。对于萨米这样的老油条而言，这绝对是一种增加指挥效率的技术。

"雷电中队长，你的飞机油量过半了，请注意。"预警机继续提醒道。

"嗯，我知道了。"萨米敷衍地回答道。

"听着，我们进行一次极限突击（不计返程油量的冲击），你们可以按照个人判断随时退出战场，也可以就近在阿拉玛伊克巴勒国际机场降落。"萨米在甚高频电台里对自己的飞行员说道，当然他的话无疑会被预警机上的伊斯拉姆少将听到。

"你们不能在国际机场降落，这……不能允许。"少将立即在电台里赶紧喊停，他无法容忍萨米自说自话地改变规则。

"听着老朋友，我的雷达已经看到了他们的长机，他就在前方15公里的地方。我占据了很好的位置，也许我可以在边界附近把他打下来。"

"不行，无论如何，你不能在国际机场降落。"

"也许我们可以活捉一个印度战斗机中队的中队长，你可以亲自审问他，想想看，辛格一定会哭鼻子的。"

萨米旁敲侧击的话似乎说动了少将，少将沉默了一会儿，没有明确地反对他莽撞的行动计划。
