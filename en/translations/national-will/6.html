Chapter 6

The Brave

The Chinese soldiers nearby looked faintly startled. The group captain thought that they had probably never seen a grey-bearded old man still flying fighters. Fair enough; Chinese soldiers did not wear beards.

"Hello," the group captain said, jumping down from the metal ladder. As he stripped off his anti-g combat vest with its dangling oxygen hoses, the onlookers saw his rank insignia. For a man of his age, the group captain did not seem especially distinguished. He shook hands with those gathered around him. The highest-ranking member of the Chinese party, he noted, was a PLA Army lieutenant colonel. This close to the Line of Control, the purpose of their visit was presumably to assess the Indian Army. The lieutenant colonel now appeared to have something to say.

"Group Captain, we heard about today's fighting. The Indian Air Force sent up Su-30s." The Chinese lieutenant colonel put his question in English. It was remarkably direct.

"They send up Su-30s every day. Nothing new about that; they just played a trick today, heh. The worst of it isn't even that. The Indian XVI Corps,1 southeast of Machhal valley, could come across at any moment. Their engineers are building roads and bridges, and there are large numbers of shallow-depth strongpoints. I've never seen them work this efficiently. But, my friend, I can't say too much. I can only report to my superiors." The group captain had mentioned the Indian ground forces deliberately. It had the desired effect: the Chinese lieutenant colonel immediately turned and exchanged a few words in private with his companions.

"Hello. I'm a staff officer with the Chinese Army. My name is Lin Huaisheng."2 The lieutenant colonel introduced himself with some formality.

"And I am the commanding officer of No. 26 Squadron. Mohammed Sami."

They shook hands again, then walked side by side past the aircraft. Sami patted the Thunder's fuselage as they passed; the skin was still hot.

"A fine aircraft. But Air Headquarters3 wants to treat her as a ground-attack platform. That's a mistake."

"You mean this little aeroplane is better suited to air combat?" Lin Huaisheng asked.

"Exactly. Though it would need more investment. An electro-optical sensor, for instance; very useful in mountain warfare. And an aerial refuelling probe. The staff people spend too much money on the Falcons' F-16s,4 and that way the Americans know our every move." The group captain seemed almost too casual, openly criticising Air Headquarters in front of a foreigner. Or perhaps he was saying all this to Lin Huaisheng quite deliberately.

"Group Captain, what about in the ground-attack role?"

"Frankly, not good. At present it can only carry 500-pound dumb bombs. There's no laser targeting pod, so it can't strike small or moving targets. The payload is too light; its sortie efficiency is certainly worse than the Mirage V."

"Hm, it really is quite small." Lin Huaisheng nodded in agreement. He knew nothing about air force aircraft, in truth; he simply felt this machine was not much larger than the J-6s or Q-5s5 one commonly saw back home.

"You have a more capable multirole fighter. The J-10,6 for example. Greater payload."

"You've been to China?"

"Every year. Chengdu, Zhuhai, and… Shenyang."7 The group captain said this with a smile.

"Ah, I see." Lin Huaisheng noticed Pakistani ground crew preparing to remove the missiles from the wingtips.

"What happened to those?"

"French equipment. Always reliable, until today. These Magic missiles had a problem; probably a fault in the seeker-head cooling system. Couldn't achieve lock. That Indian pilot was lucky. We bought these missiles a few years ago from Libya, along with the Mirage Vs. Gaddafi had the original factory batch numbers scrubbed off, so we had to take his word on the shelf life. I've been to Libya too. Their weapons maintenance leaves a great deal to be desired. Incidentally, the fuzes have been safed, but we should still keep our distance."

"Er… Group Captain, you said the Indian Army is preparing an offensive along the border?" Lin Huaisheng quickened his pace to keep up with Sami, steering the conversation back to the Indian ground forces.

"I'd imagine your satellites can see it more clearly than I can. The Indians are rushing to improve the roads in the valleys. Ordinarily they're not nearly this industrious, especially in April. Don't you find that strange?"

"Certainly somewhat strange. I've only heard that the UN envoy arrives the day after tomorrow. Perhaps a new settlement between your two countries can be reached." Lin Huaisheng brought up the United Nations deliberately; it made him appear less interested in the military situation than he actually was. In reality, he did not believe for a moment that the UN envoy could restrain the BJP's new Prime Minister.8

"My friend, if envoys were always useful, what would they need you and me for?" The group captain said drily.

"By the way, my friend, do you know a Major Song Ning?"9 Sami asked, changing the subject.

"Song Ning? Who?" Lin Huaisheng looked around at his companions. They were all shaking their heads. None of them had heard the name.

"A Chinese Air Force major currently visiting our 33rd Wing's base. He brought two J-10s for test flights. I simply thought, being in the Chinese military, you might know one another."

"Group Captain, we have an army of several million." Everyone laughed at that. Then they climbed into separate vehicles and drove off in different directions.

Northern India, Bareilly Air Force Base

Wing Commander Mohsin sat behind his desk, visibly furious. Flying Officers Goyal and Jafar stood at attention before him, clearly being dressed down.

"Flying Officer, why did you go off the air?" The Wing Commander tossed his cap onto the desk.

"Terrain, sir. The ridgeline blocked radio communications."

"Terrain? A fine excuse. Let me ask you this: do you remember the rules of engagement?" Wing Commander Mohsin pressed on. The question was an obvious trap, yet one had no choice but to step into it.

"Yes, sir. Long-range attacks only."

"Then why didn't you follow them?"

"Sir, because long-range attacks don't work. The Lions always come back empty-handed. So…" Goyal immediately threw No. 31 Squadron in front of himself as a shield.

"So you took it upon yourself to cross the border and get into a low-altitude dogfight?"

"Yes, sir. That's how it was. That's what I was thinking at the time."

"You nearly got a three-billion-rupee aircraft10 shot down by flak. And that's before we count your two necks." The Wing Commander picked up a handkerchief and mopped the sweat from his forehead. If he had actually lost a Su-30, the responsibility would fall on him too. The thought alone made him nervous.

"I didn't think of that at the time, but I know it now." Goyal answered. Jafar beside him said nothing.

"All right. Tell me about the enemy."

"Extremely cunning. When we found them, they were exploiting the terrain to fly nap-of-the-earth."

"The first one you knocked down was a Mirage III?"

"No. I believe it was a Mirage V; it looked larger."

"And the one that got away?"

"I'm certain it was a new type of aircraft. It closely resembled the Thunder, the one they got from the Chinese."

"Now that's intelligence worth studying. You're certain you didn't hit it?"

"Sir, I cannot confirm either way. When the radar lost the target, two R-27 missiles were still in pursuit, but I did not see him shot down." Goyal phrased this deliberately; it sounded as though he had scored one and a half kills. He did not want to be on the receiving end of a one-sided bollocking.

"I believe this calls for severe disciplinary action. That will have to wait until the CO returns from Bangalore, where he's attending the Tejas11 commissioning ceremony. The first priority of any military is strict discipline, not combat results. That is especially true in our 13th Wing." Goyal pursed his lips while the Wing Commander spoke with his head down. He was not hiding his contempt particularly well.

The telephone on the desk rang. The Wing Commander picked up the receiver. "Squadron commander, outside line."

"Who is it?"

"A director from the Ministry of Defence. His name's Rujadnahan."12

"The Ministry of Defence?" Mohsin had plainly never heard the name. The Ministry of Defence had never telephoned him before, either.

"No. 24 Squadron, go ahead." Mohsin took the receiver, still mopping the sweat. This April was unbearably hot.

"Oh? The Ministry of Defence?"

"Yes, sir. They're standing right in front of me. I'm currently considering the form of punishment."

"Yes."

"That's correct." Mohsin nodded repeatedly as he answered.

"What? The Defence Minister wants to see them? What is this about?" Mohsin answered, astonished.

Although Goyal could not hear the other end of the line, he stole a glance at his partner, a faintly malicious smile forming on his face. A few seconds earlier, it had begun to dawn on him that matters might be taking a turn for the better.

"The thing is, the second kill cannot be confirmed… I think… it would indeed be very difficult to verify, including the wreckage of the first aircraft, which fell on the western side of the Control Line."

"That's right, sir. The pilot reports that when they disengaged, the two R-27 missiles were still in pursuit… yes… as you say, this type of missile has a very long range… but it is a semi-active type…"

The person on the other end cut the Wing Commander off. The director was plainly not interested in an explanation of missile performance.

"Understood. I'll do as you say."

Wing Commander Mohsin slowly set the receiver down, then sat staring at the little flag on his desk for a while.

"Listen. You two idiots go and press your uniforms."

"Yes, sir."

"Tomorrow morning a Dornier 22813 liaison aircraft is coming to take you to New Delhi."

"Where, sir?"

"To New Delhi. To the Ministry of Defence. You two idiots have struck it lucky. The Defence Minister is going to present you with the Vir Chakra.14 You may even meet the Prime Minister. I find it difficult to understand, but I must say it is, of course, good news for the squadron."

"So we won't be punished, then?" Goyal asked, still pushing his luck.

Wing Commander Mohsin rolled his eyes and waved the two young men out.

To Mohsin's mind, an absurdity of this order could only mean one thing: the restless Ministry of Defence had decided to escalate in earnest. As soon as the possibility occurred to him, the Wing Commander felt the scar on his forehead begin to throb. It brought back memories of years ago, when he had been about Goyal's age, and two F-16s from PAF No. 11 Squadron15 had pursued him over the Kargil sector. He could still remember it vividly: the enemy's radar holding a continuous lock he could not shake, the piercing shriek of the Sirena16 warning system filling the cockpit, and then his desperate pull on the stick, hauling the nose up into a near-suicidal evasive break. The Sidewinder chasing his MiG-2317 could not follow the manoeuvre, but the proximity fuze detonated the warhead ten metres away regardless, and the blast shattered his canopy and left him with the scar on his forehead. To this day the scene still jolted him awake at night.

Notes

1. XVI Corps (第16集团军): The original Chinese uses 第16集团军, literally "16th Army Group." This corresponds to the Indian Army's XVI Corps, a strike corps headquartered at Nagrota near Jammu, tasked with offensive operations in the western theatre. It is one of the Indian Army's three designated strike corps. ↩

2. Lin Huaisheng (林淮生): A PLA Army lieutenant colonel serving as a staff officer. His name is composed of common Chinese given-name elements: 淮 (Huai, as in the Huai River) and 生 (sheng, "life" or "born"). His presence near the LoC as part of a military observation delegation is consistent with the Sino-Pakistani defence relationship described throughout the novel. ↩

3. Air Headquarters (空军参谋部): The Chinese 空军参谋部 literally means "Air Force Staff Department." In the PAF, the equivalent body is Air Headquarters (AHQ), Islamabad. Sami's criticism that AHQ treats the JF-17 as a ground-attack aircraft reflects a real doctrinal tension in the programme's early years: the PAF conceived the JF-17 as a BVR-capable air superiority fighter to replace the Mirage III/V and F-7, but budgetary constraints and modest payload led some planners to view it primarily as a strike platform. ↩

4. The Falcons' F-16s (猎鹰中队的F-16): See C1N10. Under the terms of the F-16 sale, the United States retained end-use monitoring rights, including the ability to inspect the aircraft and their employment. This gave Washington visibility into PAF deployments that did not apply to Chinese-origin equipment. ↩

5. J-6 and Q-5 (歼6, 强5): The Shenyang J-6 (Chinese-built MiG-19) and Nanchang Q-5 Fantan, both PLA Air Force mainstays through the 1990s and 2000s. That Lin Huaisheng uses these as his mental benchmark for aircraft size suggests a ground officer whose familiarity with air assets is limited to the older types still prevalent at Chinese bases. ↩

6. J-10 (歼10): The Chengdu J-10, a single-engine delta-canard multirole fighter developed for the PLA Air Force. Pakistan expressed interest in acquiring the type (under the designation FC-20) as a higher-end complement to the JF-17, though the deal never materialised. Its greater payload would address many of the JF-17 Block I's limitations as a strike aircraft. ↩

7. Chengdu, Zhuhai, and Shenyang (成都、珠海、沈阳): Chengdu is home to the Chengdu Aircraft Corporation, co-developer of the JF-17. Zhuhai hosts the biennial Airshow China. Shenyang is the seat of the Shenyang Aircraft Corporation. That a PAF group captain visits all three annually indicates deep institutional ties between the two air forces. ↩

8. BJP's new Prime Minister (印度人民党的这位新总理): The Bharatiya Janata Party. The novel imagines a BJP prime minister in power during the post-Mumbai-massacre escalation (see C1N8), one whom Lin Huaisheng judges the UN incapable of restraining. ↩

9. Major Song Ning (宋宁少校): A PLA Air Force major visiting PAF Base Kamra (33rd Wing; see C1N12) with two J-10 fighters for what was originally an annual exchange exercise before the escalation interrupted it. ↩

10. Three billion rupees (30亿卢比): The approximate unit cost of a Su-30MKI at the time of the novel's setting. Broadly consistent with the reported per-unit cost of Indian-assembled Su-30MKIs produced by HAL under licence, which ranged from approximately US$55–65 million depending on production batch and exchange rate. ↩

11. Tejas (光辉): The HAL Tejas (Sanskrit: "Radiance"), India's indigenous light combat aircraft. The Chinese 光辉 is a direct translation. The CO's absence at a Tejas commissioning ceremony in Bangalore, home of HAL and the Aeronautical Development Agency, places the novel's timeline close to the type's initial squadron induction. ↩

12. Rujadnahan (卢贾德纳罕): The Chinese phonetic rendering is Lǔjiādénàhǎn. As with other names in the novel, this is a transliteration that does not map precisely to any single common Indian name. Likely intended as something along the lines of "Rujadhanan"; the exact original is irrecoverable from the Chinese phonetics alone. ↩

13. Dornier 228 (多尼尔228): The HAL-Dornier 228, a twin-turboprop light transport manufactured under licence by HAL in Kanpur. The IAF operates the type in the communications and liaison role. ↩

14. Vir Chakra (勇士轮形勋章): The Chinese literally translates as "Brave Warrior Wheel-Shaped Medal." The Vir Chakra (Sanskrit: "Wheel of the Brave") is India's third-highest wartime gallantry decoration, after the Param Vir Chakra and Maha Vir Chakra. Established in 1950, it is awarded for acts of gallantry in the presence of the enemy. The chapter title, 勇士 ("The Brave"), references the decoration's name. ↩

15. No. 11 Squadron (第11中队): PAF No. 11 Squadron "Arrows," the first PAF unit to operate the F-16, receiving Block 15 aircraft in 1983 at Sargodha. In the 1999 Kargil War, the squadron flew combat air patrols along the Pakistani side of the LoC, consistent with Mohsin's memory of being chased by their F-16s. ↩

16. Sirena (塞琳娜): The SPO-10 Sirena-3M, a Soviet-era radar warning receiver fitted to the MiG-23 family. The system provided basic threat-warning capability with an audio warning tone. The Chinese rendering 塞琳娜 (Sàilínnà) is a phonetic approximation. ↩

17. MiG-23 (米格23): The IAF operated approximately 30 MiG-23MF interceptors and 40 MiG-23BN ground-attack variants, inducted between 1980 and 1985. The type struggled against the F-16 due to its inferior radar and limited missile options. Mohsin's backstory as a former MiG-23 pilot who survived an F-16 engagement explains both his habitual caution as a commander and his anxiety at the prospect of losing aircraft under his watch. The IAF phased out the MiG-23 by 2007. ↩

附近的几个中国军人的表情似乎有一些吃惊；上校想：他们大概没有看到过胡子花白的老家伙还在开飞机，没错，中国军人都不留胡子。

"你们好，"上校跳下金属梯子打了个招呼。他脱下插着氧气管的战斗抗荷背心的时候，附近的人都看到了他的上校军衔，与他的年龄相比这个军衔似乎显得不那么耀眼。上校和周围围观的几个人握了握手，他注意到这群人中间军衔最高的是一名中国陆军中校，这里离实际控制线很近，想必这些人访问的目的就是调查印度陆军。现在这名陆军中校似乎有话要说。

"上校先生，我们听说了今天的战斗，印度空军出动了苏30型飞机。"中国中校用英语提问，问题显得非常的直接。

"他们每天都出动苏30，这没什么新鲜的，只是今天玩了个花招。呵呵，最坏的情况不是这个；马查河谷东南面的印度第16集团军随时都会冲过来，他们的工兵正在修筑公路和桥梁，还有大量的浅纵深据点，我还从没见过他们这么有效率。嗯，我的朋友，我不能说的太多，我只能向上级报告。"上校故意提到了印度的地面部队，果然起了作用，中国中校立即回头与其他人私下说了几句。

"你好，我是中国陆军的参谋人员，我叫林淮生。"中校郑重地介绍了他自己。

"我么，是第26中队中队长，默罕默德萨米。"

双方再一次握了握手，然后并肩走过飞机的时候，萨米上校拍了拍这架"雷电"战斗机，蒙皮还在发热。

"很好的飞机，但是空军参谋部想把她当成一种攻击机，是一个错误。"

"你是说，这种小飞机更适合空战？"林淮生问道。

"没错，当然这需要更多的资金，比如光电雷达，在山区作战很管用；还有加油设备，参谋部的人花太多的钱在猎鹰中队的F-16上了，这样就会让美国人知道我们的一举一动。"上校说道，他看上去随和过了头在外国人面前批评了参谋部，又好像是故意在林淮生面前说这些。

"上校，对地的攻击方面怎么样？"

"说实话，不太好，目前只能使用500磅的普通航空炸弹；没有激光瞄准吊舱，无法袭击太小的或者移动的目标，载弹量也太小，出动效率肯定不如幻影V。"

"嗯，确实太小了。"林淮生点头称是，他其实不懂空军飞机，只是觉得这架飞机比起国内常见的歼6或者强5飞机也大不了多少。

"你们还有更理想的多用途战斗机，比如，载弹量更大的歼10战斗机。"

"你去过中国？"

"每年都会去。成都、珠海，还有……沈阳。"上校笑着说道。

"哦是这样，"林淮生看到一些巴基斯坦地勤人员正准备拆除机翼尖上的导弹。

"这是怎么回事？"

"法国设备的一部分，一直很管用，但是今天这些魔术导弹出了问题，可能是导引头冷却部分出了问题，无法锁定目标，那名印度飞行员走了好运。这些导弹是几年前从利比亚与幻影V形一起购入的，卡扎非搓掉了原始的出厂批号，我们只能相信他们说的保存年限。我也去过利比亚，他们的武器保养很成问题。对了，导弹引信已经解除了，我们还是离远一点比较好。"

"呃……上校，你刚才说印军正在边界上准备进攻？"林淮生一边快步跟上萨米上校，一边把话题绕回了印度陆军的部分。

"我想，你们用卫星可以看得更清楚，印度人正在抢修山谷里的道路；一般情况下，他们可没有这么勤快，尤其是在4月份。你们不觉得奇怪吗？"

"当然有一些奇怪，我只是听说，联合国的特使后天就会来了，可能会达成你们两国新的和解。"林淮生故意牵扯到了联合国，这样显得自己对战局并不那么的热衷，其实他一点儿都不相信联合国的特使能镇得住印度人民党的这位新总理。

"我的朋友，特使总是有用的话，还要你我干什么呢？"上校调侃道。

"对了，我的朋友，你认识宋宁少校吗？"萨米上校紧接着问道。

"宋宁？谁？"林淮生环顾周围几个同伴，都在摇头，显然他们都没听过这个名字。

"就是正在我们33联队基地访问的中国空军少校，还带来了2架歼10型飞机进行测试飞行，我只是想，你们在中国可能会认识。"上校说道。

"上校，我们可是有几百万人的军队。"林淮生说完，大家都笑了起来，然后各自上了不同的车，往不同的方向开去。

印度北部，巴雷利空军基地

莫辛中校坐在办公桌后面，显得怒不可遏，戈亚中尉和加法尔中尉，正笔直地站在前面，显然在挨骂。

"中尉，为什么停止联络。"中校把军帽丢到桌上问道。

"地形问题，中队长。山脊挡住了无线电通讯。"

"地形问题？很好的接口。我再问你们，你们还记得交战规则吗？"莫辛中校继续发问，这个问题就像一个明显的陷阱，但是你又必须往里面跳。

"记得，远距离攻击。"戈亚回答道。

"为什么不执行？"

"报告中队长，这是因为远距离攻击不会奏效，就像狮子中队那样总是无功而返。所以……"戈亚立即把第31中队拿来当挡箭牌。

"所以你就自作主张，穿越边界进行低空格斗？"

"没错中队长，就是这么回事。我当时就是这么想的。"

"你差点让30亿卢比的飞机被高射炮打下来，这还没算你们两个人的小命。"中队长说完拿起手巾擦了擦脑门上的汗，如果真的损失了一架苏30他也很难担待得起，一想到这些他就紧张。

"当时确实没想到，但是现在我知道了。"戈亚回答道，一旁的加法尔一声没吭。

"好了，说说看敌人。"

"非常狡猾，发现他们的时候，他们正在利用地形超低空飞行。"

"打掉的第一架是幻影III形？"

"不，我认为是幻影V，因为看上去更大些。"戈亚中尉否认道。

"逃走那一架呢？"

"我确认是一种新式的战机，很象他们从中国弄到的雷电型战斗机。"

"这倒是很值得研究的情报，你确认没有击中？"

"报告中队长，我不能确认；雷达失去目标时，2枚R-27导弹仍然在追击中，但是我没有看见他被击落。"戈亚在表达时故意耍了个花招，这样听上去就好像自己击落了1.5架敌机，他不想一边倒的挨骂。

"我想这件事必须要严厉的处罚，这得等到联队长从班加罗尔参加完'光辉'战斗机的成军仪式回来后，军队首要的是严格的纪律而不是战绩，在我们第13联队尤其如此。"中校低着头说话的时候，戈亚偷偷撇了撇嘴，显得对这些说法有一些不屑。

办公桌上的电话响了起来，中校接起了电话："中队长，外线。"

"是谁？"

"是国防部的主管，叫卢贾德纳罕。"

"国防部？"莫辛显然从来没有听到过这个名字，国防部也从来不会朝他这里打电话。

"24中队，请讲。"莫辛一边拿起电话，一边不停地擦汗，今年的4月份实在太热了。

"哦，国防部？"

"是的主管先生，他们就在我眼前，我正在考虑处罚的方式。"

"是的。"

"是这样的。"莫辛中校一边回答一边不停地点头。

"什么？国防部长要见他们？这是怎么回事？"莫辛吃惊地回答道。

尽管听不到电话里的人说了什么，但是戈亚中尉还是偷偷瞥了一眼搭档，脸上带着一丝不怀好意的笑容。几秒钟前，他开始意识到，事情可能朝好的方面变化了。

"是这样，第二个战果无法确认……我想……这确实很难证实，包括第一架飞机的残骸也掉在了实际控制线的西面。"

"对的主管先生，飞行员报告，他们脱离的时候，两枚R-27导弹仍然在追击……对……就像你说的，这种导弹的射程很远……但是这是一种半主动的……"

中队长的话被电话对面的那个人打断了，对方显然对导弹性能方面的解释不感兴趣。

"好的，我知道了，就按照你说的办。"

中队莫辛长慢慢放下了电话听筒，然后用看着桌子上的国旗发了一会儿呆。

"听着，你们两个混球回去把军服熨一熨。"

"是的，中队长。"

"明天早上有一架多尼尔228联络机来接你们去新德里。"

"去哪儿？中队长。"

"去新德里，去国防部，你们两个混球要走运了，国防部长要给你们颁发勇士轮形勋章，也许还能见到总理。虽然我很难理解，但是我必须说，对我们中队来说，当然是一件好事了。"

"也就是说不会处罚我们了是吗？"戈亚继续不知好歹地问道。

莫辛中校翻了个白眼，挥手让两个年轻人出去。

在亨纳里莫辛中校看起来，这样荒谬的事情只能暗示了一件事，心浮气躁的国防部决定要大干一场了。一想到这种可能性，中校突然就感觉到额头上的一块疤开始隐隐跳动起来。这让他回想起很多年前，当他和戈亚年纪差不多的时候，在卡尔吉尔地区被巴基斯坦第11中队的2架F-16形追击时的情形。他还清楚地记得，自己的飞机被紧追的敌机雷达连续跟踪无法摆脱，在一片刺耳的"塞琳娜"告警系统的尖叫声中，他拼命拉起机头，做出了一个近乎疯狂的闪避转弯，一颗尾随而至的响尾蛇导弹无法跟上米格23的这个动作，但是近炸引信还是在10米外引爆了导弹战斗部，炸药炸碎了座舱玻璃罩，给他留下了额头上的这块疤痕。他至今每每在梦中还会被这样的景象惊醒。
