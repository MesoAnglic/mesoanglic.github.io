Chapter 8

The Ministry of Defence

When Flying Officer Goyal woke, an AHQ Communications Squadron1 Dornier 228 had already landed at Bareilly. He and Jafar, smartly turned out, boarded the aircraft for New Delhi. Eleven days had now passed since the Mumbai massacre. The Indian government needed to produce results for an enraged public.

Bareilly was close to New Delhi; barely an hour's flight. As the aircraft descended to land at the military airfield near Delhi Cantonment,2 the flying officer could see the Air Force Museum nearby. On the apron sat a decrepit Spitfire and a Vampire, alongside some MiG-23s retired in recent years. He still remembered his school days, the first time he had come here, the instant thrill of the aces' exploits, the resolve to join the Air Force. Later he learned that the combat records in the museum were somewhat inflated. On the whole, across the various India–Pakistan conflicts, India had lost considerably more fighters in total.

After disembarking, a staff car from the Ministry drove them directly to the reception room on the ground floor. They waited approximately five minutes before a tall man wearing a Sikh turban approached.

The man introduced himself as Rujadnahan,3 office director to Defence Minister Singh. He was grave and humourless. He then raised the subject of the previous day's fighting, principally the second kill. Goyal told him he could not confirm it; only that the squadron had received intelligence indicating the aircraft had not returned to its home base. The answer made the director frown.

"Flying Officer, there's something you don't yet know. Last night, No. 7 Wing lost two Jaguars. Parliament doesn't know yet, but they will."

"Was it No. 5 Squadron's F-16s?" Jafar pressed.

"Let me finish, Flying Officer. Which enemy squadron did it is entirely beside the point. What matters is that the political establishment and the public are desperate for heroes to appear, so that they will continue to trust and rely on the Defence Ministry to resolve this conflict."

The director paused. Neither pilot had quite grasped his meaning.

"It's simple. Parliament has no patience for the complexities of aerial warfare. If you and Flying Officer Jafar are unwilling to shoulder this responsibility, I don't think it will do either of your careers any favours."4 The office director was making his meaning bluntly clear. He needed an ace with two kills, to demonstrate to the public that the enormous defence budget was paying dividends.

"If the members of the Lok Sabha5 learn that in this round of fighting we're down one to two, they'll question whether the investment of all these years has been worthwhile. You should be able to understand: every fool thinks in addition and subtraction. But the military's funding depends on these laymen and their arithmetic."

"In a moment, Minister Singh will see you. I don't think he'll raise the matter. But this afternoon, if the Prime Minister asks, you simply tell him that you clearly saw the second enemy aircraft crash in the mountains of northwestern Kashmir."

"Understood." Goyal and Jafar answered together, quietly. The director nodded with satisfaction.

"One further matter. The Minister is particularly interested in the second aircraft shot down, because he's heard it was a new type, the Thunder, and Pakistan is about to deploy large numbers of them."

"Sir, I believe this is a very, very formidable adversary. Especially troublesome in a medium- and low-altitude pursuit. I think Air Force pilots ought to be warned about this new opponent." Goyal spoke carefully, but the answer once again displeased the director.

"Flying Officer, a word of advice. You can say these things to your commanding officer or to the Chief of Air Staff. Not to the Minister. The Minister is planning a counteroffensive, and he has no use for this kind of pessimism. You must emphasise our absolute technological superiority."

"Perfectly understood." Both answered at once. Something had clicked. They had grasped, quite suddenly, everything the director's preliminary conversation was designed to convey.

"Oh, one more thing. Minister Singh has asked me to tell you both that you will each receive a Vir Chakra for this victory. Promotion to flight lieutenant will follow in a few days, naturally. After that, you have a choice. You can remain at No. 24 Squadron as front-line pilots. You can go to TACDE as an instructor. Or…" Rujadnahan stopped, deliberately leaving the bait dangling. Jafar and Goyal did not dare to guess. They waited.

"Gentlemen, the reformed No. 43 Squadron6 needs a deputy squadron commander. This new squadron will be equipped with our latest Tejas fighter. Now that is a truly formidable aircraft; certainly not some cheap Thunder. I believe that if a combat hero were willing to go, promotion to squadron leader would come swiftly."

Rujadnahan's words set Jafar's heart pounding. He knew Goyal would never choose to leave Bareilly, but for him this was a golden opportunity. In the Air Force's tradition, the back-seater's promotion prospects were always inferior to the pilot's.

"I think you're both intelligent men. You should have understood the point of what I've said. Right, get yourselves ready. The Minister will be here shortly."

Rujadnahan left the reception room and went up to an ordinary office on the second floor. Defence Minister Lakshman Singh7 stood with his back to the door, looking out at Parliament in the distance.

"They understood my meaning?" Singh asked.

"They're intelligent men, sir," Rujadnahan replied.

"Sir, this afternoon's special defence budget will certainly pass." Rujadnahan was reading the minister's mood.

"Hmph. That's only the first step. You're the man I trust most. You should know that for years I've been working toward a single goal: a permanent solution to the Pakistan problem. Ordinary politicians care only about their own advancement. They can never understand what Prime Minister Kahan8 and I are trying to accomplish. India must put an end to this unfavourable strategic geometry, and soon."

"Sir, I understand your meaning. India cannot allow 19629 to happen again."

"Only by dealing with the lesser enemy first can we concentrate our strength against the greater one. War is decided by resolve. If the enemy reaches that resolve before us, we will be ground down on two fronts." Singh turned around as he spoke. His complexion was pale, yet he radiated confidence.

"But sir, Army Headquarters may be another problem. Every plan they draw up is small-scale and timid. They fear that all-out war with Pakistan will weaken our defences facing China."

"Quite right. I know General Pasad well. He's always more timid than the Foreign Ministry. But when the moment comes, I can find someone to replace him as Chief of Army Staff."

"Speaking of the Foreign Ministry, sir, they've told me the UN may send an envoy within two days to mediate our border conflict with Pakistan. The likely choice is a former Democratic president from America."10

"Don't worry. The Prime Minister will never accept such a humiliating outcome. The Prime Minister has said it: India is the victim of terrorism. It is not for the Americans to take sides. What we're doing is no different from what they did in Afghanistan."

"But the Foreign Ministry argues that Mumbai, where the attack occurred, is 1,700 kilometres from the conflict zone in Kashmir. They consider persuading the international community to be a headache."

"Not surprising. The Foreign Ministry is always like this."

"Additionally, sir, Pakistan claimed this morning that aircraft rehearsing nuclear strikes had crossed their border. The Foreign Ministry is afraid this will mislead international opinion."

"Hmph. Utter nonsense. They think having one permanent Security Council member in their corner is enough. We have the other four. The Foreign Ministry should make proper use of our diplomatic resources. I really don't know what they're so afraid of."

"On that subject, Secretary Chandra telephoned, asking about the crash locations of the two Jaguars. I told them we're still investigating. One pilot is missing."

"That is a thorny matter. If the pilot has fallen into their hands, there will be problems. Before we go to open war, we have to tread carefully." Singh said.

"On this matter, I must brief you on a new development," the director said. Singh nodded for him to continue.

"No. 7 Wing's morning report states that one of the Jaguars had indeed been modified to carry nuclear weapons.11 If the self-destruct system failed to function, the cockpit computer systems would contain current attack route data, including pre-programmed nuclear delivery profiles. That could serve their propaganda. Additionally, there is information on forward airfields and IFF codes. If it falls into their hands, it would cause serious problems for the air force at the front."

"Could the pilot still be alive?"

"Sir, it's April. A night on that ground — he's probably frozen to death." Rujadnahan said this carefully.

"Hm. Still, don't be complacent. Telephone Northern Command.12 Have them send a patrol to search. At the very least, destroy the wreckage. Right, then. Time to meet those two young men."

The Defence Minister pushed open the door to the reception room. The two pilots in their crisply pressed uniforms sprang from the sofa to attention. They had been holding their breath for nearly fifteen minutes, and now the moment had arrived.

"Gentlemen, splendid. Tell me — what does it feel like to shoot down the enemy?" Lakshman Singh asked with a smile. The expressionless director stood behind him. Goyal thought: this is a moment where mistakes are not permitted.

"Like a fireball in the sky, sir."

"Hm, a fine image. It reminds me of 1971. I was a tank gunner with the 19th Armoured Division then. I put a round into the turret of an M48 and watched it burn. Exactly that kind of spectacle."

"We will shoot down every enemy aircraft that intrudes," Jafar said, chest out.

"Good. Just like that. Now tell me about this new enemy aircraft. Pakistan has been boasting about this new type for some time. How was it?"

Jafar glanced discreetly at Goyal, hoping he would field this one.

"An unremarkable light fighter. Whether within or beyond visual range, utterly no match for us." Goyal answered with practised ease. But in the instant he spoke, what rose before his eyes was the pale blue aircraft turning above him, clear enough to make out the two missiles on its wingtips — clearer, somehow, than it had been in the fight itself.

"We're short on time. Get ready, gentlemen. This afternoon we have to go to Parliament. The Prime Minister needs you. I hope your heroic accomplishments will knock some sense into those left-wing blockheads in the Lower House."

Notes

1. AHQ Communications Squadron (空军总部特勤中队): The Chinese renders this as AHQCS and gives 空军总部特勤中队, literally "Air Force Headquarters Special Duties Squadron." In the IAF's organisational structure, AHQ Communications Squadron is the unit responsible for VIP and priority transport, operating light aircraft such as the Dornier 228 and HS 748. The HAL-Dornier 228 is a twin-turboprop light transport manufactured under licence by HAL in Kanpur. Its use to ferry two junior officers from Bareilly to New Delhi is consistent with standard IAF practice for high-priority personnel movements. ↩

2. Delhi Cantonment (德里坎登门): The Chinese 坎登门 (Kǎndēngmén) is a phonetic rendering that most plausibly corresponds to "Cantonment." Delhi Cantonment encompasses a large military zone in southwestern New Delhi, adjacent to Safdarjung Airport and the Palam military area. The Defence Ministry is housed in South Block on Raisina Hill, several kilometres to the northeast. The nearby Air Force Museum at Palam displays retired IAF aircraft including Spitfires, Vampires, and MiG-23s, consistent with the author's description. ↩

3. Rujadnahan (卢贾德纳罕): See C6N12. The character functions as the Defence Minister's chief political fixer, coaching the young pilots on what to say and what not to say. His role in this chapter is to ensure that the narrative presented to Parliament aligns with the Ministry's strategic objectives rather than with operational reality. That he wears a Sikh turban, combined with his employer being "Defence Minister Singh," establishes both men as Sikhs. ↩

4. Kill claims and political pressure: The director's demand that Goyal claim two confirmed kills, when the second remains unverified, parallels a recurring phenomenon in the history of aerial warfare: the inflation of kill claims for political purposes. The tension between what pilots know to be true and what their governments need them to say is a theme the novel explores through Goyal's growing discomfort. The Indian side's exchange rate in this engagement stands at one confirmed kill against two Jaguars lost. ↩

5. Lok Sabha (人民院下院): The lower house of India's bicameral Parliament, directly elected by the public. The Chinese 人民院下院 literally renders as "People's House Lower Chamber." Defence budgets and war authorisations require Lok Sabha approval, giving its members direct leverage over military funding. The Defence Minister's concern over their perception of defence spending underscores the political pressure driving the military narrative throughout this chapter. ↩

6. No. 43 Squadron / Tejas LCA (43中队 / 光辉): IAF No. 43 Squadron "Ibexes," historically a transport unit. The novel fictionalises it as a fighter squadron being reformed with the HAL Tejas (see C6N11). In reality, the first Tejas squadron was No. 45 "Flying Daggers," raised at Bangalore in July 2016. Rujadnahan's characterisation of the Tejas as categorically superior to the JF-17 Thunder represents the political rather than the operational view; at the time of the novel's setting, the Tejas was still in protracted development and had not yet achieved initial operational clearance. ↩

7. Defence Minister Lakshman Singh (国防部长拉什曼·辛格): A fictional character. The name "Lakshman" (Rāma's brother in the Rāmāyaṇa) combined with "Singh" suggests a Hindu or Sikh identity. His background as a 1971 war veteran gives him military credibility and a personal stake in conflict with Pakistan. He bears no direct resemblance to any specific Indian Defence Minister, though his desire to escalate recalls the political atmosphere during the 2001–2002 India–Pakistan standoff. ↩

8. Prime Minister Kahan (卡汗总理): The Chinese 卡汗 renders as Kǎhàn. Given the novel's depiction of a BJP government riding a nationalist wave, the name is likely intended as a Hindu given name rather than the Muslim "Khan." The precise Indian equivalent is difficult to recover from the Chinese phonetics; "Kahan" or "Kalhan" are plausible readings. See C6N8 for the novel's broader political configuration. ↩

9. 1962 (1962年的事情): The 1962 Sino-Indian War, in which China inflicted a decisive defeat on India along the Himalayan border. The reference encapsulates India's foundational strategic anxiety: the fear of being caught unprepared on the Chinese front. Singh's argument is that Pakistan must be neutralised first to avoid a two-front predicament, a strategic logic that has recurred in Indian defence planning since the war's aftermath. ↩

10. A former Democratic president (美国民主党的一位前总统): Given the novel's timeline (post-2008), this most likely alludes to Bill Clinton, who undertook diplomatic missions to North Korea and elsewhere during the Obama administration, or possibly Jimmy Carter, who has a long history of international mediation. The detail establishes the novel's geopolitical backdrop: the United States attempting to de-escalate a crisis that the Indian government has no intention of resolving peacefully. ↩

11. Nuclear-capable Jaguars (挂载核武器的改装): The IAF Jaguar has long been identified as one of India's nuclear-capable delivery platforms. India's nuclear doctrine relies on aircraft-delivered gravity bombs as one leg of its triad. The revelation that a downed Jaguar contained nuclear delivery profiles and IFF data significantly raises the stakes: if Pakistan were to recover this material, it would constitute both a propaganda coup and an intelligence windfall. The missing pilot and wreckage become a crisis within the crisis. ↩

12. Northern Command (北方军区): The Indian Army's Northern Command, headquartered at Udhampur in Jammu and Kashmir, responsible for all military operations along the LoC and the Line of Actual Control with China in the northern sector. Singh's order to dispatch a patrol to find the wreckage reflects both the intelligence sensitivity of the downed Jaguar and the political necessity of controlling the narrative before Pakistan can exploit it. ↩

戈亚上尉起床的时候，空军总部特勤中队（AHQCS）的一架小型的Do 228联络飞机已经降落到了巴雷利机场。他与搭档加法尔整装一新，乘坐着这架飞机前往新德里。这一天距离孟买的恐怖袭击已经11天了，印度政府需要拿出一些战绩来向暴怒的人民交待一下。

巴雷利基地离新德里很近，飞机只不过一个小时的路程。当飞机降低高度准备降落在国防部位于德里坎登门附近的军用机场时，中尉可以看到，附近的空军博物馆，紧邻德里机场的停机坪上，停着一架老掉牙的喷火式战斗机和吸血鬼战斗机，另外还有一些最近几年淘汰的米格23，他还记得中学的时候，第一次来到这里，当即被空战英雄的事迹所感召，决心加入空军，后来他知道，博物馆里所记述的空战记录有所夸大，总的来说，在历次的印巴冲突中，印方损失的战斗机总数要多得多。

他们下飞机后，国防部派来的专车直接将其送到了国防部一楼的接待室，等了约5分钟，一名头戴锡克教头巾的高大男子走了过来。

来人自我介绍叫卢贾德纳罕，是国防部长辛格的办公室主管，显得严肃又不苟言笑。随后他主动谈起了昨天的战斗，主要是第二个战果方面的，戈亚中尉告诉他自己无法确认，只是中队得到情报表明这架飞机没有飞回他起飞的机场；这个回答让主管皱了皱眉头。

"中尉，有一件事你还不知道，就在昨天晚上，第7联队损失了2架美洲虎，这件事国会还不知道……但是总会知道的，"

"是第5中队的F-16干的？"加法尔追问道。

"请先听我说完，中尉。是敌人的哪个中队干的，这一点儿都不重要，重要的是，政界和人民都非常期待一些英雄出现，这样他们就会继续信任和依靠国防部来解决这一次的冲突。"

主管说完停了一会儿，两名飞行员都没能很准确地领会他的意思。

"事情很简单，国会没有耐心了解空战的复杂性，如果你和戈亚中尉不愿意承担起这样的责任，我想，这对二位的前途不会有任何的好处。"办公室主任非常露骨地向戈亚与加法尔表明了他的意思，他需要一名击落2架敌机的王牌飞行员，来向民众表明，大把的国防预算是有回报的。

"如果人民院下院的议员们听说在这一轮交锋中，我们处于1比2的落后当中，他们就会怀疑这些年的投资是不是值得，你们应该能够理解，所有的傻瓜都是用加减法来想问题的。但是军队的资金取决于这些外行的审核。"

"待会儿，辛格先生会和你们见面，我想他不会过问这件事，但是今天下午，总理如果问起这件事，你尽管告诉他，你清楚地看到了第2架敌机坠毁在了克什米尔西北部山区。"

"我们知道了。"戈亚和加法尔一起轻声回答道。主人满意地点了点头。

"还有另一件事，部长阁下对被击落的第2架飞机很感兴趣，因为他听说是一架新式的雷电战斗机，而巴基斯坦即将部署大量的雷电战斗机。"

"主管先生，我想，这是一个非常、非常难缠的对手，尤其在中低空的追逐中非常麻烦。我觉得，有必要让空军飞行员都警惕这个新的对手。"戈亚说道，这个回答再一次让主管不太满意。

"中尉，我有一个忠告，你的这些话可以对你的联队长或者是空军参谋长说，但不能对部长说，部长先生正在策划反击，所以他不喜欢这一类消极的看法，必须强调我们在技术上的绝对优势。"

"完全明白了。"戈亚和加法尔同时回答，他们突然开了窍，突然领悟了这名主管的事先谈话中想要传达的所有要领所在了。

"噢对了，辛格部长让我转告你们二位，你们每个人都会因为这次胜利得到一枚勇士轮形勋章；上尉军衔么，当然稍晚几天也会提升。然后，你们可以选择继续留在24中队当尖子飞行员，或者去TACDE（空战学校）当一名教官，或者么……"卢贾德纳罕停了下来，故意卖了个关子。加法尔和戈亚不敢多猜，都等着他说下去。

"诸位，重新组建的43中队缺少一名副中队长，这个新生的中队将要装备我国最新的光辉（LCA）战斗机，那才是一架真正难缠的战斗机，决不是什么廉价的雷电战斗机。我相信，如果有一名空战英雄愿意去的话，很快就会提升为少校。"

卢贾德纳罕的话让加法尔的心怦怦乱跳，他知道戈亚一定不会选择离开巴雷利，但是这对自己是一个绝好的机会，在空军的传统中，后坐的武器操作员的升迁机会往往逊于飞行员。

"我想二位都是聪明人，应该已经明白我说的重点了，好了准备一下，部长马上就会来了。"

卢贾德纳罕走出接待室，来到了二楼的一间普通办公室。国防部长，拉什曼辛格正背对着大门，看着远处的国会大厦。

"他们都明白了我的意思了？"辛格问道。

"他们都是聪明人，阁下。"卢贾德纳罕回答道。

"阁下，今天下午的特别国防预算案，一定会通过的。"卢贾德纳罕察颜观色道。

"哼，这只是第一步。你是我最信任的部下，你应该知道，我这些年都在致力于一件事，就是一劳永逸地解决巴基斯坦问题；普通政客只知道钻营自己的私利，永远无法明白我和卡汗总理的苦心，印度必须尽快结束这种不利的地缘格局。"

"阁下，我知道您的意思，印度不能再让1962年的事情发生了。"

"只有先解决了次要的敌人，然后才能集中力量对付更强的那个。战争取决于决心，如果敌人比我们先下了决心，我们就会被他们在两个方向上拖垮。"辛格转过身子说道，面色苍白却又充满了自信。

"但是阁下，陆军参谋部可能是另一个麻烦，制定的所有方案总是小打小闹，他们害怕对巴基斯坦的全面开战会削弱我们对中国方向的防御。"

"没错，我很了解帕斯阿德上将，他总是比外交部更软弱，不过关键时候，我可以找个人来接替他当陆军参谋长。"

"对了，说起外交部，部长阁下，他们告诉我，联合国可能会在2天内派一名特使来调解我们和巴基斯坦的边界冲突，人选很可能是美国民主党的一位前总统，外交部似乎很想就此平息冲突。"

"放心好了，总理不可能接受这样屈辱的结局，总理说过，我国才是恐怖主义的受害者，轮不到美国人来拉偏架，我们所做的和他们在阿富汗所做的没什么两样。"

"但是，外交部认为发生恐怖袭击的孟买，离克什米尔冲突地区有1700公里，外交部认为说服国际社会是一件头疼的事。"

"不奇怪，外交部总是如此。"

"另外，部长阁下，巴基斯坦方面今天早上说，有一些演练核攻击的飞机穿越了他们的边界，外交部很怕他们误导了国际舆论。"

"哼，简直是一派胡言，他们以为有一个联合国常任理事国可以撑腰，我们有另外四个。外交部应该把我国的外交资源充分利用一下，真不知道外交部到底在怕什么？"

"是这样的，钱德拉秘书打电话来，问了两架美洲虎坠毁的位置。我告诉他们还在调查中，有一名飞行员失踪了。"卢贾德纳罕如实回答道。

"确实是一件棘手的事，如果飞行员落在他们手里，确实有一些麻烦，正式开战前我们还得处处小心。"辛格说道。

"关于这件事，我想我必须汇报一些新的情况。"主管说道。辛格点了点头示意他说下去。

"第7联队早上的报告，其中一架美洲虎飞机确实做过挂载核武器的改装，如果自毁装置没有起作用，在座舱的计算机系统内，可以找到最新的攻击路线资料，有一些是预设的投掷核弹的线路；这会对他们的宣传起作用。另外，还有一些诸如前线机场和敌我识别装置的信息，如果落到他们手里，也会对前线的空军造成很大的麻烦。"

"飞行员有可能还活着吗？"

"阁下，现在是4月份，掉在那里一晚上，很可能已经冻死了。"卢贾德纳罕小心地说道。

"嗯，还是不要掉以轻心，你去给北方军区打个电话，让他们派一个小分队过去去找找看，至少把飞机残骸摧毁掉。好了，现在是去见见那两个年轻人的时候了。"

国防部长推门进入接待室的时候，两名穿着笔挺制服的飞行员立即从沙发上站起来立正，他们几乎是屏息等了15分钟，现在这个时刻到了。

"小伙子们，太好了，说说看打下敌人的感觉怎么样？"拉什曼辛格微笑着问道，面无表情的主管站在了他的身后，戈亚想这可是一个不能出错的时刻。

"就象一个空中的火球，部长先生。"

"嗯，很好的比喻，这让我想起了1971年，当时我还是19装甲师的一名坦克炮手，我打中了一辆M48的炮塔，看着它燃烧起来，就是这种壮观的情景。"

"我们将击落所有入侵的敌机。"加法尔挺胸说道。

"很好，就是这样，说说看，那架新出现的敌机，巴基斯坦方面吹嘘了很久这种新式飞机，怎么样？"

加法尔悄悄向戈亚转了转脸，希望他来回答这个问题。

"乏善可陈的轻型战斗机，无论是视距内还是视距外的空战，完全不堪一击。"戈亚很从容地回答道，不知道怎么的，说话的瞬间，他眼前浮现的，正是那架浅蓝色的飞机在他上方转弯的情景，清晰到可以看到翼尖的两枚导弹，好像比他在实战中看得更清楚。

"时间不多了，小伙子们准备一下，下午我们还得去一趟议会，总理需要你们，希望你们的英勇战绩，多少能让下院那些左翼分子的榆木脑袋开窍。"
