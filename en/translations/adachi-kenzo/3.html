Chapter Three: A Ramble Through Korea and the Sino-Japanese War

from the autobiography of Adachi Kenzō.

1. First Inspection of Korea; Consul-General Murota, Minister Ōishi, General Kawakami Sōroku

Around this time Korea presented phenomena deserving of close attention, such as the stirrings of the Tonghak party and the measures of the Korean government; in particular, the attitudes of Russia and Qing China toward Japan, with Korea at the centre, and the general international situation in the East had begun to show signs of turning grim and pressing, so I constantly maintained a keen interest in the various problems of the Orient and never neglected their study.

In late March of Meiji 26, Sassa-sensei planned a ramble through Korea, and I accompanied him along with Sassa Masayuki. The ship first arrived at Pusan, where the consul-general of the day, Murota Yoshiaya, was stationed. He was a native of Mito, and since both Sassa-sensei and I were devotees of Mito learning, we met with him and found ourselves greatly of one mind. That interview became the occasion for a deepened friendship in later years.

When we went on to Keijō, Ōishi Masami was stationed there as minister to Korea, and at that time the so-called grain-embargo affair was in dispute between Japan and Korea, with Minister Ōishi in the midst of hard-line negotiations with the Korean government. The grain-embargo affair was this: around Meiji 22, in the Wŏnsan region, Japanese merchants had been buying up rice when the Korean government abruptly promulgated a grain-embargo order, that is, a prohibition on the sale of rice to other countries, blocking the Japanese merchants' purchases; the Japanese government had been demanding of the Korean government compensation for the losses thereby incurred. The Korean government, however, adopted one delaying expedient after another and the talks made no progress whatever. Minister Ōishi had reopened them after taking up his post in January of Meiji 26, but they drifted along as slippery and inconclusive as ever, until the exasperated minister at last, in mid-May, gave notice that negotiations had broken down, made ready to withdraw to Japan, and resolutely hauled down the Hinomaru flying over the legation. The King of Korea, observing this from the palace, sensed that matters had grown serious, abruptly changed his attitude, dispatched an urgent envoy, and agreed to accept our demands. As I recall, the indemnity we demanded had been whittled down and down over the course of the negotiations until the final figure was a mere 110,000 yen. When I think back now on those days, when the question of handing over or withholding so trifling a sum stood as a diplomatic impasse from Meiji 22 onward and came at last to the hauling down of the national flag, it truly feels like another age.

Sassa-sensei's stay in Keijō with us ran to two weeks, during which we observed conditions in every quarter in detail. As for future policy toward Korea, my view was that Japan had no course but to take China as a friend and join hands with her against Russia, and to that end the Eight Provinces of Korea should be divided in two, the north made a Chinese sphere of influence and the southern half placed under Japanese protection; that is, I framed a plan for the partition of Korea. Japanese policy toward Korea in those days was passive and timid, accomplishing nothing, and among the genrō and senior statesmen there were even those who argued for abandoning Korea and being rid of the nuisance, so my partition thesis counted as quite a bold forward position.

On the return journey, at the Suizu Inn in Inch'ŏn, I chanced upon Chief of the General Staff General Kawakami Sōroku and met with him. The general was then mid-journey, attended by the finest talents of the General Staff, Tamura Iyozō, Shiba Gorō and others among his staff officers, intending to inspect Keijō and proceed thence to Tientsin and Peking. Through this meeting I became acquainted with General Kawakami.

2. Yuan Shikai and Sassa-sensei

At the time of our Korean ramble, the Japanese resident in Keijō numbered fewer than a scant five hundred, while the Chinese, counting police patrols alone, reached two thousand: the very zenith of the era of Chinese ascendancy. And the illustriously reputed Yuan Shikai was stationed in Keijō as their representative. He would have been thirty-four or thirty-five then, and in the vigour and edge of his prime he behaved exactly as he pleased. Sassa-sensei engaged Yokota Saburō, a clerk of the Keijō consulate, as interpreter and met with Yuan Shikai; I too sat in on the meeting.

One day the three of us, Sassa-sensei, Masayuki and I, hired Korean ponies and climbed Pukhansan. The inscriptions on the mountain were overwhelmingly concerned with defence against the wakō, and indeed it would hardly be amiss to say that not Pukhansan alone but every famous mountain and old fortress everywhere in the country was built as a precaution against the wakō. All of it bespoke how deep and how ancient are the ties binding our country and Korea.

3. Inoue Kowashi-sensei's View of Siberia; the Storm over Korea Turns Suddenly

In Meiji 27, the education minister Inoue Kowashi-sensei, stricken by illness, was convalescing at Hayama in Shōnan. He had, it seems, long since heard of my aspiration to study Eastern questions, and one day he summoned me to his sickbed at Hayama. He said: "Until now our Japan has slept facing the Pacific, but the day has come when she must turn over in her sleep. That is, she must turn from the Pacific to face the Sea of Japan. Korea goes without saying; I believe we must give earnest consideration to the Russian question. Now, I have great sympathy for your study of the Eastern question, and I should like to lend you some measure of support. For the present you may set Korea aside; study Siberia first. You ought to cross to Siberia at once and reconnoitre the Amur, ascending it as far as you can."

With that he granted me three hundred yen as a subsidy toward travel expenses. I thanked Minister Inoue for his kindness and at once negotiated passage with the mail steamship company, haggling over discounts and the like. I remember that just as I was hurrying to the company's offices by rickshaw there came a violent shock: this was the great Nōbi earthquake. While I was thus completing my various preparations for the Siberian journey, the storm over Korea grew suddenly acute: the Tonghak insurrection had broken out. Taking this internal disturbance as pretext, China hurriedly dispatched land forces and stationed them at Asan in Ch'ungch'ŏng province. Thereupon our own cabinet council likewise resolved to send troops in the name of protecting our residents, and the order to mobilise came down to the Hiroshima Composite Brigade. Minister Ōtori, then home in Japan, hastened back to his post in Korea aboard the warship Yaeyama.

Sassa-sensei, seeing this sudden turn of events, warned me to call off the Siberian journey and urged that now was the moment to hasten to Korea. Which of my two seniors, Inoue or Sassa, was I to heed? Not a little perplexed, and thinking there might be some way of bringing their intentions into accord, I visited Inoue-sensei's sickbed at Hayama once more. Being confined to bed, sensei had of course not been attending cabinet, and so knew nothing of the dispatch of troops to Korea. When I reported in detail the circumstances under which the cabinet had resolved on sending troops and the course the Korean crisis was taking, and explained fully that seeing Korea must now come first, sensei gave his ready assent; and my two seniors being thus brought to agreement, I switched over decisively to preparations for crossing to Korea and hastily made ready for the road.

4. Second Inspection of Korea; General Kawakami's Letter of Introduction to Chief of Staff Nagaoka

By the time I set out, the Hiroshima Composite Brigade had already departed, so I called on Chief of the General Staff Kawakami, heard from him the general situation, received a letter of introduction to the brigade's chief of staff, Nagaoka Gaishi, and at last, on the thirtieth of June, Meiji 27, took the road to Korea. Crossing over, I found the Japanese army encamped at Yongsan in a posture of standing by, with no sign whatever of advancing. I went to the temporary barracks at Yongsan, promptly presented General Kawakami's letter, and met Chief of Staff Nagaoka. And I passed the several weeks until the army opened operations, each day dragging like a thousand autumns, in one room of a shabby Japanese house rented in Keijō.

5. With the Army to Asan; the Sweltering Heat of Sosajang; the Camp at Sŏnghwan; Paeksŏkp'o

Now, when the order to advance came down to the Japanese army waiting at Yongsan, I was to observe the campaign as a special war correspondent of the Kyūshū Nichinichi Shimbun. Operations began immediately on the twenty-sixth of July; the first day I lodged with the army at Suwŏn, the second at Chinwi. Around noon of the third day we reached Sosajang, where the army halted and waited for nightfall. Sweat streamed, our throats felt scorched with thirst, yet there was no water to drink; we were reduced to sipping the water pooled along the banks of the paddies to relieve our thirst by the barest measure. That night we bivouacked with the troops at Sosajang, and from midnight the army began to move. In the small hours of the twenty-ninth the curtain rose on the famous battle of Sŏnghwan. The ground at Sosajang was slightly elevated, a pine wood on the right running out ahead, and the low ground before us all paddy fields. The Sŏnghwan stream flowed through the middle, and a single road crossed the bridge over it and led to Sŏnghwan. The battle of Sŏnghwan, the overture of the Sino-Japanese War, opened across this brook. One of our platoon commanders gave the order to deploy and charge from behind the stream, and as his men plunged headlong into it to press the attack, it was unluckily high tide and the water deep; some soldiers, unable to bear the weight of their kit, were even drowned. But under the diversion that unit provided, the other platoons all crossed the little bridge, slipping through the enemy's furious fire, and charged as one, winning at last a great victory and putting the Chinese troops to rout. Our army too suffered some casualties, and the celebrated death in battle of Captain Matsuzaki came at this moment.

With several fellow correspondents I went into the Chinese camp at Sŏnghwan, and out of the litter of abandoned gear I secured a single folding fan as my trophy. The most comical thing at the time was this: one of the men with us spotted a tin lying about, smacked his lips certain it must hold biscuits or some such, drew his sword and cut it open, when, who would have thought it, out flowed a black, viscous liquid. It was opium, that favourite of the Chinese. Going on another dozen or so chō, we discovered a Chinese soldier lying hidden among the paddies, and Sugi of the Jiji Shimpō and I chased him down between us and took him prisoner without difficulty. While we dawdled in this way the main body of the Japanese army advanced, and we, marching as it happened with the unit of the fallen Captain Matsuzaki, lost our way; march as we might we could not come up with the main force, and were sorely at a loss. The platoon commander then halted, studied his map, and said: the main body is probably on the far side of that mountain to our left, so we should make straight in that direction. Proceeding thence along the ridges between the paddies, we came upon a small hamlet just as a Korean family was at its evening meal, and were given a share of their rice to still our hunger. Crossing the mountain along a small ravine, we at length reached Yoran, where the main force was halted, and managed to join it at dusk.

From drinking foul water and eating oriental melons through days of blazing heat I at last came down with diarrhoea, showing symptoms of the so-called Korean dysentery. Thoroughly sick of the overland route, I considered returning to Inch'ŏn by sea. Hearing there was a sailing from Paeksŏkp'o to Inch'ŏn, the next day I went with several fellow correspondents to Paeksŏkp'o, and as we waited for the boat to arrive we ran into some Chinese, still ignorant of the fall of Asan, putting into port in a Korean vessel laden with foodstuffs and the like. A small guard detachment and the correspondents together seized it, and after various interrogations confiscated the entire cargo. Presently a small steamer arrived; I boarded it, landed at Inch'ŏn, and thence got back to Keijō. There being no further need for me to observe the fighting, I resolved to return to Tokyo, partly to make my report, and to plan a fresh venture.

6. Meeting Viscount Shinagawa on the Way Back to the Capital; Report to General Kawakami

On the way back from Keijō I stopped at Hiroshima, met Viscount Shinagawa, who was staying at the villa of a certain man of wealth at Ujina, and then went straight on up to Tokyo. On arriving I went at once to the private residence of Chief of the General Staff Kawakami Sōroku, met the general, and reported the state of the fighting. When he had heard my report through, he said, his face wreathed in satisfaction: "Last year I met Sassa and yourself at Inch'ŏn, then went to Keijō and had audience of the Korean sovereign, and thence to Tientsin, where I also met Li Hongzhang. In the middle of my talk with Li Hongzhang, I had the intuition: we have already won." He spoke like a man unable to contain his delight; and I surmise that General Kawakami, in conversing with Li Hongzhang, had detected some opening in the man somewhere. Such, truly, is the eye of the soldier: it was with that full confidence, one gathers, that he directed the present campaign.
