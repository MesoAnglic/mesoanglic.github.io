In Place of a Preface

On the Publication of Adachi Kenzō-sensei's Autobiography

This autobiography of Adachi-sensei is a posthumous manuscript, written down as occasion prompted after he had withdrawn into retirement, and afterwards compiled by the editor. Bringing it before the world had been the editor's cherished wish for many years, but until now no opportunity offered itself. This time the newspaper company too has stepped back from the front line and gained a little leisure, and so, seizing the chance, I resolved to publish at last this autobiography that had long been my heart's desire.

Adachi-sensei left great footprints upon the history of our party politics; he was an eminent party statesman. To the world at large he is famous as the god of elections. But was Adachi-sensei a man whose whole figure should be summed up as the god of elections? No, decidedly not. His aspiration lay, from first to last, in the statecraft of the nation. The origin of the nickname "god of elections" goes back to the general election under Ōkuma's cabinet in the third year of Taishō, when Adachi-sensei, as chief of elections for the Rikken Dōshikai, wielded the baton ably and the ruling party won a sweeping victory. At the time, Prime Minister Ōkuma's popularity was seething throughout the country; and on that occasion Tokutomi Sohō-ō, in the columns of the Kokumin Shimbun, confirmed the ruling party's victory and praised the masterly baton-work of Adachi the election chief, writing "Truly, Adachi is the god of elections."

Ever since, the nickname "god of elections" became Adachi-sensei's signboard as a politician. To be fair, he did love elections, was zealous about them, and was skilled at them; he really was a god of elections, and he himself would smile wryly at the label. Yet his true calibre lay elsewhere, and one reading of this autobiography will make that plain.

Adachi-sensei was born in Kumamoto in the first year of Genji. His boyhood, youth, and prime spanned Japan's passage from its dawn into its ascendancy, an age of nationalist development; he breathed the thought currents of that era and grew up within its national atmosphere. From boyhood he studied the Chinese classics at the Tomoeda academy and received the tutelage of Sassa Katsudō, being tempered by the Confucian spirit; from this his moral outlook and worldview were fixed, and here the steely backbone of Adachi Kenzō was formed. From boyhood to the end of his days, his dream lay in the statecraft of the nation.

His activity as a party man was, in a word, majoritarian; but the majority was a means, and never the end. He formed majority parties in order to carry out statecraft, to further the nation's development and secure the stability of the people's livelihood. As Minister of Communications he did not stop at invigorating ordinary communications administration: shipping being then in the doldrums, he poured his energy into reviving the maritime world, and to animate the American routes he had the mail steamship company build three new vessels. The company, grateful for the Minister's ardent backing, affixed the syllables of his surname, A, TA, CHI, to their names: the Asama-maru, the Tatsuta-maru, and the Chichibu-maru.

During his years as Home Minister he carried out one undertaking after another: the development of broadcasting, the advancement of aviation, the spread of simplified life insurance; and he bent such passion toward the extirpation of tuberculosis and the relief of leprosy that within the ministry he earned the nickname "Minister of Hygiene." Again, at the founding of NHK, the business circles of Tokyo, Osaka, and Nagoya were strongly inclined to organize it as a joint-stock company; against this he insisted upon the public character of broadcasting and carried through, decisively, the present structure of an incorporated association. The general public, dazzled by his great election victories, tended to overlook these administrative achievements; yet all of them attest that he was no mere god of elections, and that his true province lay in the governance of the state.

Adachi-sensei was also a progressive conservative. That he took the lead in advocating universal suffrage, and that as Home Minister he showed deep sympathy for the women's suffrage movement, are one or two proofs of his progressive thought. After parting ways with the Minseitō he later organized the Kokumin Dōmei, becoming its president, and held a lofty and independent position in the political world. Where the true mettle of Adachi Kenzō showed itself, however, was in the cooperative-cabinet question. The high-handedness of the military was growing ever more extreme, and he saw through to the fact that, left unchecked, it would one day bring fearsome consequences upon the nation; only the strength of national unity, he judged, could rein in the army's excesses. Though he stood as vice-president of the Minseitō, served within the cabinet as a Home Minister of quasi-vice-premier rank, and was assured of the next party presidency and the premiership, he could not be reconciled with Prime Minister Wakatsuki; and his plan to organize a cabinet of national unity in cooperation with the Seiyūkai was founded on the conviction that, setting aside all regard for his own person and household, this was the best possible course for the nation. Matters turned out contrary to his intent and, by ill fortune, the attempt ended in failure; yet the aspiration itself is imperishable. Even judged against the subsequent course of the nation's affairs, one must acknowledge that Adachi-sensei's penetrating eye had seen clean through the evils of the age.

In his last years he gathered charitable contributions and poured out his own fortune to erect the Hasseiden in Yokohama and the Sankendō in Kumamoto, out of a heartfelt wish that they serve as training halls for the moral cultivation of the people. After withdrawing into seclusion at the Hasseiden and the Sankendō, he fretted constantly over the state of the times, sighing that mediocre men were leading the country astray. He gave particular care to the education of the people's sentiments, pouring his passion into the promotion of shigin recitation. Viewed from one side, this may be taken as an expression of Adachi-sensei's religious and pedagogical temperament. The Hasseiden, moreover, was donated to the City of Yokohama, and the Sankendō to the City of Kumamoto. Incorruptible by nature, he was the very model of a party politician. Whoever reads this autobiography through will surely come away with a renewed recognition of Adachi-sensei's true character.

Lastly, on the occasion of this book's publication, I offer my deepest thanks to Mr. Komorida Kazuki and Mr. Makino Takeo for their kind cooperation.

18 June, Shōwa 35
Izu Tomito
