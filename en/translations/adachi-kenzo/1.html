Chapter One: Childhood

from the autobiography of Adachi Kenzō.

1. The Adachi House and the Shibuya House

The Adachi family was originally called Adachi, written as 足立, and in Kan'ei 9, when the Hosokawa house entered Higo from their former domain of Kokura in Buzen, the family came along in their train. The name was later changed to the Adachi written 安達. As for what circumstances during the Kyōhō years caused the change, it is not known. There were as many as four Adachi households in Kumamoto. Their descendants flourished and branched out, and twice a year, in spring and autumn, this same-surname fellowship-gathering was held. My own Adachi house, though it may be called a separate lineage, attended that meeting all the same.

In our Adachi house there was a successor, an eldest son, yet for some reason it ended up without an heir and faced extinction; so an adopted son was brought in from the Gotō family to carry on the Adachi name, and that adopted heir was my father.

My father's name was at first Uzaburō, later changed to Nihei. The original surname of the house was Shibuya. Its ancestors too had followed a provincial lord, coming from Kokura to Kumamoto. The Shibuya house served the Hosokawa for generations as falconers, in the so-called lower-retainer station of minor vassals. Coming from a falconer's rank, various men issued from it; my grandfather was called Kenji. My grandfather knew my grandfather's father well. In my youth, when I asked Yamada-san about the family, the house finally died out completely.

The mother's eldest brother, that is to say my uncle Ichitōta, served at the Hosokawa lord's accounting office and was posted on Edo duty. He was a quite capable, promising person, my mother said, and his voice when receiving and entertaining guests was much remarked upon. My mother told me my own voice and intonation are exactly like brother Ichitōta's, and in later years she would say so at every turn; she clearly mourned this brother of her own flesh who died young, and recalled him on any occasion. They were very alike. She listened intently while I spoke, and somehow it seemed precious to her; perhaps that was it.

2. A Father of Many Tastes, Who Loved Nature

My father was fond of calligraphy, painting, and antiques, and when young he was a man of truly many tastes. He loved to paint and did it well; he loved nature; and as for household poverty and the like he kept it entirely out of mind. He especially cultivated and took delight in chrysanthemums and irises. Among these he studied the so-called Higo chrysanthemum under Mr. Sakurai, the adopted father of Mr. Arima Gennai, and so prized them that he came to be known for what people called the Higo chrysanthemum bed, famous enough to be spoken of that way. When I was twelve or thirteen, I accompanied my father.

There was an incident, and when Yamada-san asked about my birth-house, my father answered that he was the grandson of Shibuya Kenji.

Nihei was born the second son of Kenji, but because the eldest son went into seclusion owing to illness, whether he would take up the hawk himself, or whether he should lead hunting-dogs and accompany the lord, that duty he did not let himself be bound to, and so he raised a family name, ceding matters to his younger brother Kanji and himself taking from the Shibuya house, which at the time had become an extinguished house.

In olden feudal times, the army's organization was divided into spear, bow, gun-and-cannon, and so on, and that division depended not on the man but on the house. The Adachi house belonged to the bow-corps. The origin of their becoming bow-side dates to the Hōei years, when a man named Adachi Tsu'uemon was, per a record, entered specially into the inner bow-corps by separate register on account of his archery skill. Because such a record exists, and the Adachi house succeeded to the bow-corps, my father down to his youth had the bow implements remaining stored in quantity in the Adachi house, and a few of these were my playthings as a boy.

My mother's father was called Okumura Kaisuke. He too, like the Adachi, was of the very minor-retainer gentry. The Okumura house came from Tonomura in Shimomashiki district. Their residence was likewise in Kumamoto city; when I was five or six I was taken along by my mother and went back and forth to that house any number of times, which I remember. My mother had two elder brothers called Ichitōta and Nizōta. Ichitōta succeeded to the Okumura house, and whether through some irregular circumstance, or because the heirs of both houses generally died young, these things were many.

3. A Father Who Loved the Wildflowers of the Roadside

He often went eel-fishing too, and on those occasions thoroughly relished the fine art of it. With that, my father became quite the angler; alone he would readily go out, with a skilled fisherman's bearing, and being good at it he often went by himself. Sometimes, at the stone embankment of a bridge spanning an irrigation channel at Igate in Gotomi village, in Kumamoto dialect ide, an irrigation watercourse, where in later years he rushed about on official duties driving himself hard, passing over that bridge I would feel just as if meeting an old acquaintance.

To this day, one keepsake of my father's I treasure and preserve is an old, small single-stem flower vase. This vase, when my father accompanied the Hosokawa lord on the alternate-attendance journeys, on the road to and from Edo, he never let it leave his person, keeping it cherished against his breast. On the three-hundred-ri long journey, why he carried such a thing might seem odd, but my father, on the road, at the lodging near each night's stop, would take it out, pluck a roadside flower, some nameless wildflower blooming by the way, set it deftly in the vase and place it in the tokonoma, and silently, quietly, sit gazing into it, passing the lodging-night of his travels. He was always intimate with nature, and never forgot to love it.

4. Memories of Mother

The person who was my mother, that is, the third daughter of Tsunoda Ichitōta, so I am of the cousin relation to that line.

The Kusano house was in Takebe-machi. From the Kusano house, some ten-odd chō away, there is Tatsuta-yama nearby, and so from time to time, after the family moved house to the new residence, my mother, being invited, would go out together with them, to gather pine-needles to eke out the household's fuel-supply. And so I too went out then for pine-needle raking; though these excursions were made to relieve a poor household's economy, on the way home, with the pine-needles and dead branches we had gathered bundled in rough rope and slung on each back, descending the mountain path, there is something of that. And on the homeward road, my mother's figure seen from behind, bearing it, even now stirs in me an inexhaustible feeling.

A child, as a child, has appropriate desires; plans too. When I was eight or nine, recalling those days, for a child, broaching a desire or a plan, opening it up to a parent, is somehow daunting, somehow hard to bring out. And so the one I confided my heart to was always my mother. My mother would sympathize with that consultation and skilfully season it, arrange it, and then later present it to my father and obtain his permission; that is how it always went. My mother's capacity to feel keenly, even now, for a child's emotional life, was truly great; a virtuous wife and wise mother.

My mother had no broad learning or book-cultivation to speak of, but she was most richly endowed with common sense; her disposition was generous and magnanimous, calm in all things, never clinging to the vulgar, thorough in her good sense and bearing, and she looked after people widely and well in their dealings. Whatever the relatives might be, if anything came up I would at once rely on my mother, and take this and that to consult with her. Relatives aside, after the Seinan War, in a corner of a farmhouse in Shimochikami village, Hōwa district, we lodged temporarily.

And from then on too, the households of the nearby farm families, if asked, my mother would go anywhere to help, going out and going off. There came a time when cholera broke out in the Kumamoto region, and even within the village it spread, and many fell ill in succession. As it happened the head-housewife of the house caught it. Then, in her final stage, my mother, morning and night, with her own hands drew water and took no rest at all, and never minded the danger of contagion in the least, nursing her, and at the very end she gave the water from her own mouth to the dying woman's, they say. Whether one calls it kindness, whether one calls it care, when help reached someone it was truly overflowing with warm feeling; that was its quality.

5. From the Temple-School to the Primary-School Era

When I turned nine, I first went up to what was called the terakoya, the temple-school. The master was a man named Kaneko Wazō. Going there, the lessons ran from the handwriting of the iroha up through reading-matter and on to the plain reading-aloud of the Analects. As subjects, besides penmanship, there were single-character primers, the Kankō tsūgen by Mr. Uehaba Katsue, and the like, as I recall. They also had us recite the Song of Right Spirit in chorus, and taught us recitation. In particular its preface and such were made to be sternly memorized; the line "I am imprisoned in a north garden, sitting in a one-knight cell, eight shaku wide, some four hiro deep". At the time I understood nothing of its meaning; I simply memorized the cadence and chanted it loudly. As it happened, I committed it to memory. Now, even today, I have it by heart.

In those days I grasped none of the meaning of the Song of Right Spirit's preface, only its rhythm; yet that is precisely why I memorized it, declaiming it loudly. The encouragement of poetry-chanting given by the various teachers of that time, to it I am grateful for the influence and the deep impression of my childhood years. In Jōgyōji-machi there was an old sake-brewery building belonging to a place called Kawachi-ya; the school being there, after about a year it was closed.

Having renovated it, it was named the Jōgyōji Primary School, and there the schools were merged. A man named Kōyama Suehaya, as head teacher under the Kumamoto Normal School first cohort of graduates, implemented the new teaching method. I graduated from that school, and for superior results was awarded by the Kumamoto prefectural governor Mr. Yasuoka Ryōsuke the "Physics Ladder, Part One, three volumes". This was in Meiji 5.

That same year, Meiji 5, there was Emperor Meiji's imperial progress through the Kyūshū region, and on the nineteenth of June he proceeded to Suizenji in Kumamoto city. I was led by the hand by my mother, and we hurried at a trot to the present Ōe-machi, along the Suizenji road, reaching the third Igate nearby; the Emperor was on horseback. At the time, it was said that to look straight upon the Son of Heaven and worship him full-face would burst your eyes, such an age it still was; yet I worshipped him, and I remember being deeply moved.

At eleven, the School Ordinance had not yet been promulgated, but the school that may be seen as the forerunner of the present primary school was set up by borrowing the temple Kuhonji in Tatsuta-guchi-machi, and I attended that school too. Its founder was Nakamura Yoshiyuki, a marital relation on my mother's side, that is, the husband of the second daughter of Tsunoda Ichitōta, together with Mr. Saido. The school at that time was a special pre-Ordinance school. At the school then there were Inoue Keijirō, Morita Yutaka, Narita Yasusada, Ijima Tadaji and such; these were the head pupils and pupil-supervisors, but the one now still living is Mr. Inoue alone.

My same-class fellow pupils at the time were six boys and two girls, eight in all; and of the boys, the pair Furukawa and Isogai dropped out partway, and the remaining four had the following ranking. That is, in the order Itai, Haruno, Adachi, Itagaki, these four were the first cohort of graduates. As for myself at the time, I rather thought I had done quite well; but Itai and Haruno, the two of them, were truly capable men, and in the end I could not surpass them, passing through in third place. Regrettably, these two gifted students died young one after another in later years, and the others too have all already passed from the world. And so, when I dimly look back on my childhood, I feel all the more keenly that nothing is more important than minding one's health.

Counting on my fingers, it was about sixty-five years ago now; Meiji 9 was just the year of my primary-school graduation. When I left the school, that very October, the disturbance of the Kumamoto Keishintō, that is, the Shinpūren, broke out, led by Ōtaguro Tomoo, Kaya Harukata and others. In the dead of night flames rose at the barracks and elsewhere, and gunshots could be heard incessantly. I remember going outside with my mother, gazing at the flames from afar, and shivering all over.

6. Refusal of Appointment as Prefectural Office Attendant

The former-samurai of that time had their hereditary house-stipends taken away, and since they had to clothe and feed themselves on the interest of a scant monetary-stipend bond, they were for the most part in extreme poverty. And so the sons of the gentry, in order to supplement the household economy, mostly tended to regard being taken on as attendants at the prefectural office and the like as rather an honour. When I too graduated from primary school, I received, through my class teacher, an overture as to whether I might be taken on as an attendant to Governor Yasuoka; but I flatly declined it. And when I returned home and reported this to my parents, my parents too seemed to be satisfied. When I think that this refusal of attendant-appointment in my childhood years was the first step in my conduct of advancing and withdrawing in public life, I could not help feeling a deep inner satisfaction.

7. Memories of the Seinan Campaign

When I was fourteen, in Meiji 10, I lived through the Seinan War, the uprising of Saigō Takamori. The Satsuma army set out in mid-February from Kagoshima, defended at Tabaruzaka and the Kichiji Pass, and in early March had already pressed upon Kumamoto Castle and besieged it. Then they came south, and the government army marching down; over more than two ten-day-spans, day and night, fierce attack-and-defence unfolded; this I recall. A few days before the government army, in their thousands, came surging into Kumamoto city, the castle's defenders, resolved on a siege-defence, set fire and burned away the greater part of the city's downtown. So-called clearing the field, sweeping clean to await the coming enemy. The famous Kumamoto Castle, wrapped in the flames, black smoke piercing the heavens, that terrible spectacle I can even now summon vividly to mind.

The new-residence quarter facing across the Shirakawa, on the first occasion, did not catch the soldiers' conflagration, so my family's first evacuation stayed put at our house. Seeing the situation in which the government army, having landed in the Hinagu and Yatsushiro direction, took Matsubashi and Uto and was gradually closing in on Kumamoto, Major Oku, later Field Marshal Oku Yasukata, was made to lead a battalion under his command and break through the encirclement near Yasumi-bashi, in order to link up with Commander Tani Tateki, who was holding out under siege in the castle. Since the vicinity of my family's house was all stricken by the fires of war, we then withdrew toward the Tabaruzaka direction to Kengun village, which had been arranged in advance as a second place of refuge. But then Kumamoto Castle's siege was lifted.

The Satsuma army, from Kengun village toward the Hota-kubo direction opening onto the high ground, tried to attempt a last stand; unexpectedly this village turned into the very centre of fierce fighting, so we and the householder together took refuge in a hollow of the open moor near there, spending one night under the open sky. Along the road running to Kawajiri-machi, the present national-highway route, at a place called Shimochikami in Hiyoshi village, the Shibuya household too was sheltering, so once the campaign ended we relied on the Shibuya house and moved house in that direction.

8. The Era of Temporary Lodging at Shimochikami Village

Although I say we moved to settle in Shimochikami village, there was no house to rent, so from a certain farm family, Monji Jirō by name, of a middling-or-better farmer's level, we borrowed the parlour of the farmhouse to lodge in for a time; and since the family was small, we acted as a sort of caretaking household. The house was to be built in great haste; there was talk of that, and we promptly answered it and went. In truth it was a single eight-mat room, and even so it was a splendidly post-built little hut. The surrounding walls were mud, plastered by hand to the four posts; the roof was of course thatch, but not a single iron nail was used; they bound the timber with rope; and although I call them four posts with the kamoi lintel-rails run across, the dwelling was a primitive relic, no proper dwelling at all. From today's view one could scarcely even imagine it. Yet my parents went on living there a full eight years or so. In front of that post-built hut my father tilled the land of Master Tomoeda, the college's land, and built a flower-bed, growing chrysanthemums and irises and roses and such, cultivating flowering plants unceasingly through the four seasons, taking his pleasure in it.

9. Household Finances

As for the household economy, my father was entirely unconcerned, indifferent, leaving all of it to be managed by my mother's single hand. Still, my father had a kind of firm conviction, which at times appeared as instruction and teaching. It was: never once borrow money. Never pawn a thing. Do not complain of want. Eat earth and drink water to live, and even at that you should live actively, holding firm to a maxim of getting by, and amid the turbid, muddy current keeping yourself free of great error. He held to a worldly precept of standing apart, set in the muddy stream yet committing no great fault, and for this I feel grateful.

How did the Adachi house manage to make a living? In the early years of Meiji, a public bond was issued for the former-samurai class to settle their hereditary house-stipends. What our house received was, on the face of it, 695 yen, with interest at seven shu a year. This seven-shu interest came annually to 24 yen 32 sen 5 rin, received in two instalments a year. And this 24 yen 32 sen 5 rin was, for a household of three, parents and child, more or less, the half-year living expense. However one reckons it, there was no way it could suffice. Naturally, from nearby farm families and the like, my mother was asked to do tailoring-piecework, and by sewing won the wages of that needlework, a meagre income to help eke out the household; and I cannot help recalling the ordinary-and-yet-extraordinary toil of my mother.
