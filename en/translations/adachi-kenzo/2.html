Chapter Two: Student Days

from the autobiography of Adachi Kenzō.

1. Entering the Ninsai Gakusha; the School Rules

My fourteenth year passed emptily amid the tumult of Saigō's war, but at fifteen, since a teacher of Chinese learning named Tomoeda Shōzō was running a private academy, the Ninsai Gakusha, in Tamana village, Tamana district, about seven ri north of Kumamoto city, I too came to enrol in this school. As to why I should go out of my way to enter a country academy from Kumamoto: the truth is that the natal family of this teacher's wife was called the Fukuhara house. And after the mistress of the Fukuhara house, Mrs Tomoeda's own mother, had recently died, a daughter of my grandfather of the Shibuya house, that is, my father's younger sister (to me an aunt), went to them in marriage as the second wife, so that a roundabout tie of kinship arose with my own house as well. Through this connection I had long been told of Tomoeda-sensei. Since the Fukuhara house was at a place called aza Shimomura, Bairin, Tamana district, I first went to the Fukuhara house in my aunt's company, and after a stay of a day or two proceeded under her guidance to Tomoeda-sensei's home, formally entered the academy, became one of his disciples, and settled down to study.

Tomoeda-sensei's academy stood at the eastern edge of Tamana village along the Takase River, the lower course of the Kikuchi, one ri from the town of Takase, an exceedingly tranquil spot. His educational policy took the way of filial piety as its foundation and was in all things one of practice and personal enactment. The school rules, too, were in the style of the Three Articles of the Law; there were only two provisions.

First: lying and private chatter are not permitted. Second: desks, books and the like must not be trodden on or stepped over. Third: whoever violates the above shall be sentenced to three days of salt fare.

As to what manner of punishment "three days of salt fare" was: if one violated either the first or the second article, then for the following three days, at all three meals, morning, noon and evening, one was given nothing whatever with one's rice except salt. And this was enforced with genuine rigour. As for what feasts the students ordinarily ate: the rice was half rice and half millet, so much so that one might as well say we ate nothing but yellow millet; soup was miso soup once a week; the rest of the side dishes were ground miso, zazen beans, salted sardines and the like.

The school fee was ten sen a month. Gratuities to the teacher were not specifically prescribed, but the sons of the wealthy farmers of the district mostly presented bales of rice at the year's end. I gave two yen each year-end, but those two yen were by no means easy to come by, and there were times when I privately sold off the Shiji, the Zuo Commentary and other books handed down in our house to raise the money for the gratuity. As for lessons, in the daytime each studied as he pleased; at night the teacher's lectures were held, and we were taught the Elementary Learning, the Analects, Mencius, the Zuo Commentary, the Book of Odes, the Recorded Words and Deeds of Eminent Song Ministers and so on. While at the academy I visited my parents in Kumamoto only about once in two months, and trudging the seven ri of hill and field, crossing Tabaruzaka, famous from the War of the Tenth Year, on the way home is likewise a happy memory.

When I had been at the Tamana academy a little over a year, Tomoeda-sensei's aged mother was living outside Kumamoto city in Rikigō village, in those days Shimashin village, aza Susukiba, and in order to render his filial duty to her the teacher wound up the Tamana academy for a time and removed to this Shimashin. My own home was in nearby Hiyoshi village, close under my father's knee, but I nonetheless followed the teacher to his Shimashin academy, and in the end, across Tamana and Shimashin together, I received his guidance and moral influence for fully six years all told; what I gained from it was truly immense. That debt to my teacher I cannot forget even now.

2. The Epitaph of Tomoeda-sensei

Alas! Here rest the mortal remains of my friend Tomoeda. My friendship with him was of the closest, and my grief for him runs deepest of all. Whenever I sought to compose his inscription, a hundred feelings would gather at once and I would cast down my brush and stop. Now the grass on the path to his grave has thrice renewed itself, and his disciples press me without cease; so, enduring the pain, I write.

His personal name was Shigetaka; he went about as Shōzō; his surname was Tomoeda, and the family lived for generations in Shimashin village, Akita district, Higo. His father's name was Keisuke; his mother was of the Naitō family. Five sons were born to them. When the father died, the master, the fourth son, was given in adoption to the Tajiri family. The adoptive father and mother of the Tajiri house were both living, and he served them with a filial obedience no different from that toward the parents who bore him. It happened that troubles arose in that house; he shouldered the hardship and swallowed the bitterness, exhausting his strength to mend matters. At times he came to me to take counsel, and his utter, aching sincerity moved me to tears every time. When the Tajiri mother passed from the world, he resumed his original surname.

He studied at the gate of Kinoshita Ison-sensei. In reading he sought to penetrate the great import of a book, disdaining the fussy philology of chapter and phrase, and gave himself with particular devotion to the study of history. Whenever he discussed the gains and losses of past and present or weighed the worth of men, he never failed to bring the whole room to conviction.

Thereupon he went to Tamana district and gathered students to teach. One village of the district was likewise named Tamana, and there lay the site of the former residence of Ōtsuka Taiya-sensei, who in the Kyōhō era, by pure virtue and solid practice, had been the exemplar of the province and its villages. The master had long admired his manner, and so purchased the land and built upon it a garden he named Kasei, "Fit to Dwell In." Those who came to study under him increased by the day. One day his longing for his mother grew past containing; he put his affairs in order and made ready to return to his village. The people said in alarm: "Sensei, your main family is already rich in means, and your brothers are many. In the observances of warmth and coolness, in the offerings of sweet and nourishing food, she lacks nothing; surely her care does not wait upon you. If you wish to see your honoured mother, we shall bring her here by palanquin several times a month; or, failing that, we may go to her in your company. Only stay; do not go." He shook his head and said: "Between nearest kin there dwells an utmost feeling that others cannot know. My mind is made up." He then took his household home and made his dwelling next door, just south of the main house. Morning and evening he paid his attendance, anticipating her wishes and reading her countenance, and mother and son lived in perfect contentment. And still the villagers of the old garden at Tamana sent their sons and younger brothers to study with him there.

Presently his mother fell ill; he gave up sleep and food alike and nursed her down to the smallest particular. When she died, his grief and self-mortification passed the proper measure; his perfect filiality was surely inborn. When the mourning ended he returned to live at the old garden in Tamana. The villagers congratulated one another, and those coming to study grew more numerous still.

He taught with earnest devotion, taking personal practice as the foundation and making his students fix their wills on being men of purpose. To young beginners he gave the strict warning: "Speak no idle word; whisper in no ear." At night he would summon three or five of them to pound his back and knead his hips, and as they did he would talk with them of the classics and histories, touching also on the affairs of the world, making it his business thereby to nourish their natures and draw them forward in learning. Sometimes, deep in the night, he would send them into desolate shrines and ruined temples in the mountains to test their nerve and courage. Thus all who passed through his gate knew their bearings and did not stray; even in a dense crowd one saw their speech and carriage and knew without asking that they were his disciples.

He lay ill for years. The day before he died, knowing he would not rise again, he said to his disciples: "Since I took to my sickbed, it is you few who have attended the physicians and the medicines; you few also who have helped me rise and rest and prepared my food and drink. Your care has been earnest and thorough, your service scrupulous and reverent, three years as a single day. Even the eminence of kings and lords could not assure such a thing, yet I alone have obtained it. How fortunate I am, to die in your hands." As he finished speaking he smiled gently. The disciples all wept, and none could lift his eyes to him. When he died, from the bathing, dressing and shrouding of the body to the bearing of the coffin and the raising of the funeral banners, the disciples did everything. His burial place is a great mound several hundred paces northwest of the garden, from which one may gaze into the distance; a certain villager divined the site for him and saw to its arrangement. Alas! He died at the hands of his few disciples and was buried by the hands of his few disciples; surely he is without regret.

He lived at Tamana some ten-odd years first and last, and knew to the full the sufferings of the villages. If he saw a village clerk playing the tyrant, he would without fail rebuke him to his face and grant no quarter; wherever the people's weal and woe were at stake he spoke out forthrightly. Above all he took as his own charge the upholding of the cardinal bonds and the maintenance of the moral order of the age. The province and its villages were transformed by his influence, all binding and cultivating themselves; even the wild and unruly, hearing of his manner, changed their countenance. On the day of his death there happened to be a festival at the village shrine, and every house was receiving guests and feasting; at the news all put away their wine and raised the cry of mourning. Far and near, those who knew him and those who did not, none failed to sigh in sorrow.

He once rebuilt the village shrine, and his stewardship of it was exceptionally thorough. As a commissioner of village preparedness he laid up stores against famine; in suppressing rioters he brought the whole village peace; elected vice-chairman of the prefectural assembly, he won the quiet assent of public feeling. All of this, surely, flowed from the overplus of his virtuous conduct.

At first he seemed to pay the affairs of the day little mind. But when political argument rose up on every side and perverse, inflammatory talk injurious to the Way spread across the land, he rose in indignation and said: "I have already lost the one who was my heaven; there is nothing left for which I need preserve myself." From then on he rushed east and west, braving cold and heat, exhausting his strength in the work of rectification and rescue; and it is said it was through this that he contracted his illness.

In person he was imposing and remarkable, tall and stately, his voice high and clear. All his life he held himself upright and incorruptible, taking not the smallest trifle from another. With old acquaintances who had climbed onto the path of office he ceased even to exchange letters; with me alone was he a friend without the least discord of heart, and a gift from me he never once refused. When I lived in Ikura we were separated by a bare twenty li. In leisure hours I would take my wife and children to visit him; I would call to him across the river, and he would at once pole a small boat over to meet us. We would laugh together in delight, and only after a stay of two nights would I turn home; when he came to me it was just the same. We always counted this our utmost pleasure. After I went out to serve in office in distant parts we could no longer meet often, but our letters passed back and forth without fail at the seasons of the year. Hearing he had fallen ill, I sent word advising him on the care of his health and promised to return and visit him in his sickness; but in the end I could not fulfil it. Alas! He did not fail me, yet I failed him. What is one to say?

He was born on the third day of the first month of Tenpō 11 and died on the seventh of December, Meiji 20, aged forty-eight. His wife was of the Fukuhara family; he had no children. The inscription reads:

The teacher's Way declines by the day; the crowd is drunken, the multitude astray.
Alone there was my friend, unashamed to be a teacher of men.
He planted peaches and reared plums; filiality alone was his foundation.
He hated evil as a foe: one great shout, one blow of the staff,
and wasps and scorpions sheathed their venom, rats and dogs drew in their necks.
How strong, how unbending! The courage of the benevolent man.
The mountain of Shōdai: his lofty integrity may stand beside it.
The waters of Takase: his stainless purity is their like.
He followed the tracks of the former worthies; the exemplar is here.

Composed by Seisei Koji, Takezoe Kōkō.
Written by Kazan Sanshi, Mizuno Yukinao.

3. Entering Seiseikō; Exemption from Tuition; A Ramble through Hichiku

In Meiji 16 I transferred from Tomoeda-sensei's private academy, the Ninsai Gakusha, to Seiseikō, run by Sassa Tomofusa-sensei. Transfer I did, but paying Seiseikō's monthly tuition of fifty sen was, in our household circumstances of the time, a matter of considerable difficulty, and I was at last holding off attending, when Sassa-sensei went so far as to dispatch Asayama Tomosada-sensei to my house to urge me to come to school. And when on that occasion the economic circumstances that kept me from attending became known, the school decided specially to exempt me from tuition.

As for how I came to receive such recognition and favour: before this, while I was still enrolled at the Ninsai Gakusha, the various schools and private academies of Kumamoto had banded together and held joint examinations from time to time. In the examination hall the students of the several schools and academies were gathered in one room and, before the assembled teachers, made to perform recitation-readings of the Four Books, the Elementary Learning, the Nihon Gaishi and the like. Since I had attended these examinations as a student of Tomoeda-sensei's Ninsai Gakusha, and moreover as its head boy, Sassa-sensei had come to know that there was a certain Adachi at the Tomoeda academy, and it was partly owing to this that I came to receive such special treatment from Seiseikō.

It was the summer of the year I transferred. One day Sassa-sensei called me to him and recommended that I use the summer vacation to travel about between Higo and Chikugo and broaden my knowledge of the world. Thereupon I set out with alacrity in the company of Tsutsumi Masato (later an army major; died of illness). There survives the Diary of a Tramp through Hichiku which I wrote at the time. I excerpt its substance here by way of commemoration.

"August, Meiji 16, the year mizunoto-hitsuji. At the time of the Seiseikō vacation I engaged with my schoolmate Tsutsumi Masato for a tour of Hichiku, and on the 13th we made ready our gear and set forth. It chanced to be the season of lingering heat, scorching wind and fiery cloud, exactly as though we sat within a furnace, and we took our road over the steep of the Kichiji Pass. A gasp at every step, sweat streaming like jewels; the hardship of the ascent was at its utmost. After a while we gained the summit, took the shade of the tall pines and drank the pure air; gazing round on every side we cried 'splendid!' and cried 'marvellous!'; unawares I recalled the campaign of the Seinan War and ten thousand feelings churned my breast. Beholding Sannotake thrusting sheer into the blue sky, I cherished the thought of the Kumamoto seniors' hardships in their stubborn defence; hearing the old pines sighing in the west wind, it was as though the battle-cries of day and night had never ceased; emotions gathered from every quarter, beyond restraining. My tears fell and became the rain of Shinohara. Alas." (Shinohara Kunimoto, general of the Satsuma army, fell in battle at this spot.)

At four in the afternoon we passed Ikura town, called on Kunitomo-sensei for a brief conversation, and further, by way of Takase, arrived at dusk at Tomoeda-sensei's Kaseien, that is, the Ninsai Gakusha where I had pursued my studies in former years, and lodged the night. On the 14th we took leave of Tomoeda-sensei, passed through Kanayama village to Yamaga station, at last broke across the prefectural border, and in the afternoon arrived at Miike town. Prior to this, on setting out, Sassa-sensei had furnished us with letters of introduction to the men of spirit, or shishi, of Hichiku, so the plan was to call on them along our route.

From Miike we came out at Miyabe village and lodged a night at the home of Yufu Kurō of that village; on the 15th we took leave of that house, passed through Watase and Enoura, crossed the Yabe River, passed Nakashima village, arrived at Yanagawa town and worshipped at the Mihashira Shrine. At noon we called on Nagamatsu Takeshi and Soga Yūsei, and after a little, under Nagamatsu's guidance, met Watanabe Murao, conversed further with Mori Gunji, Katayama Moichirō and others, and that night lodged at Watanabe's house.

On the 16th we called early in the morning on a certain Takagi, an old retainer of the Yanagawa domain, passed some hours, parted also from Watanabe, and headed for Saga by way of Enokizu town. At noon we crossed the lower course of the Chikugo River and printed our first step within Saga prefecture. Some two ri from Ishizuka village, at three in the afternoon we undid our travelling gear and lodged at the temporary residence of Takahashi Nagaaki in Ryūtaiji Lane below Saga castle.

On the morning of the 18th we worshipped at the grave of Edayoshi-sensei, elder brother of Soejima-sensei, whom I had long revered as a great man, and in the afternoon, braving wind and rain, called on Taketomi Tokitoshi. That night too we imposed upon the Takahashi house.

On the 19th we called on Hanabusa Shigeharu and Ikebe Kichitarō and in the afternoon returned to the Takahashi house.

On the 20th, at nine in the morning, we took leave of the Takahashi house and set our road on the Kurume highway; passing Kanzaki station and Kokeno village, we turned right to Mamezu station, crossed the Chikugo River to reach Senoshita town below Kurume castle, and a dozen-odd chō further attained the city of Kurume. We called on a certain Ishino, Morotomi Shintarō and others, and that night lodged at the office of the Chikusui Society.

On the morning of the 21st we visited the Mizuma district office; the district head was Yokomakura Kakusuke, and among the clerks were Matsuzaki Jirō, Torii Shūsaku and others. In the afternoon we worshipped at the grave of Takayama Hikokurō, called besides on Arima Kōzaburō, president of the Chikusui Society, and that night lodged again at the society's office.

On the 22nd we left Kurume for Akizuki, passed Nankun village and came out at Miyase station, reached Hongō town at noon, and thence, tracing a byroad west of Amagi station, entered Akizuki, called on Itō Eijirō and lodged at his house.

On the 23rd we at last turned homeward: Hongō town, the Tenmangū at Kitano, crossing the Kumashiro ferry, worshipping at the Kōra-san Shrine, descending the mountain road to seek out the relics of Takayama Hikokurō at Fuchū town, and further in quest of these calling at the house of Mori Sengo of Kōradai village; thence a stage of two ri brought us to Fukushima station, where we lodged a night.

On the 24th we left Fukushima early in the morning, took the new national road from Kanamatsu, entered Kumamoto prefecture, arrived at Yamaga town at four in the afternoon, proceeded to Bunda village and lodged at the house of Satō Keita. Murakami Ichirō also came and joined us.

On the 25th we took leave of the Satō house and, from Mitori station, by way of Ueki town, arrived back in Kumamoto at noon and entered Seiseikō.

The above was the beginning of my wanderings beyond the prefecture, and I remember that what I gained on this journey was very great indeed——

At the Seiseikō of those days, each room of the boarding house (holding some fifteen to twenty men) had a room head, and above the room heads, as heads of the students, stood a head student and a vice-head. At the time of my transfer, Kinoshita Uzaburō (later an army lieutenant-general) held the position of vice-head, but as he aspired to the Military Academy and was to transfer to the Seijō School, which might be called its preparatory school, I succeeded him as vice-head. The vice-head was allowed two yen a month for board, later raised to two yen fifty sen.

After a while as vice-head I became a head. The heads at that period were three, Minoda Kitarō, Asayama Kageharu and myself, and later Ichishita Tadashi also became one. The head was in origin simply a single student: though chief of the students, he was supposed to be no official of the school. Yet before anyone knew it the office had become bureaucratised and come to grasp the powers of school administration, while the post of dormitory superintendent had been reduced to the holding of a mere empty vessel. Thus for a time the powers of school administration came to be handled at the heads' pleasure, and among the staff there were apparently some who criticised this sharply; even Mutō-sensei once remarked with irony, "The heads here are above the stewards."

At Seiseikō I did not pursue the regular curriculum as such, merely auditing lectures as occasion offered. Sassa-sensei had early recognised the urgency of German studies and promoted them above all, with such fervour that he went to the length of drawing Yamagata Ryōzō-sensei (a man of Yamaguchi prefecture) from Tokyo as teacher of German. I too, following his encouragement, was one of those who studied German. As formal coursework I audited in particular Iida Kumata-sensei's lectures on the Doctrine of the Mean and Nakanishi Ushirō-sensei's on the Public Law of All Nations.

4. Infantry Drill, Firing Exercises, Rabbit Hunts and the Like

The adoption of military-style drill into school education was likewise something Sassa-sensei zealously advocated, and in having it incorporated into the curriculum and carried out, Seiseikō was probably the pioneer among all the middle schools of the nation. I applied myself to this military drill with exceptional zeal. Of course not one student in those days wore Western clothes; the costume was Japanese dress with tube sleeves and hakama; but I, because Asayama Tomosada-sensei had once been in the army, received from him a sergeant's old tunic and put it on over hakama, a getup so outlandish that to think of it now I can scarcely keep from laughing, and at times took command of the drill as deputy instructor. As instructor came Captain Numata Kuhachirō, who had been adjutant to Major-General Nogi, commander of the Kumamoto division (afterwards the war-god General Nogi), with a sergeant as his assistant. One day I, likewise acting as unit commander, had the students circle the Yamazaki parade ground several times at the double; in the end stragglers dropped out one after another, and that everyone was mightily done in by that double-time remained a topic of talk decades afterward.

Such being the state of things, I could muster little interest in kendō and never put my hand to it; but Seiseikō's kendō, for its part, was vigorously promoted after Okamoto Genji took up the post of dormitory superintendent, thereafter advanced flourishingly, and Seiseikō became, in kendō too, one of the most distinguished schools in the country. The mass fencing practised today was likewise instituted in Okamoto's time. In later days, when I had taken my seat in the cabinet and made an inspection visit to Seiseikō, watching this mass fencing, no sooner did I hear the sound of that drum than Okamoto's figure rose vividly before my mind.

In those days no school anywhere had conducted anything like a firing exercise, but I, having gained confidence through infantry drill, was bursting with ambition and could not rest until I had tried it once in the field. So I resolved to attempt it when occasion offered: I myself became umpire, made Ide Saburō and Ōno Kenjirō the two commanders-in-chief, divided the students into northern and southern armies subordinated to each, marched out to the heights of Kuroishibaru and held a firing exercise, applying our everyday training to attack and defence on actual ground. This venture was in truth the very first student firing exercise in Japan. When I recall the scene of those days I forget my seventy-seven years and feel the blood grow hot in my veins.

The rabbit hunt has from feudal times been famous as an observance peculiar to the Kumamoto region, but its revival after a lapse, and its great invigoration, were also entirely Seiseikō's doing. Men connected with the old Yoneda house of the Takebe quarter, who knew thoroughly the lore and fine points of the hunts of former days, instructed us in them. And we would mobilise the whole student body for marches of a day, or of one or two nights out, hunting rabbits along the way and so drilling ourselves in the field. Thus the Seiseikō rabbit hunt became famous and gradually spread to the other schools of the prefecture. Excursions too were held from time to time, in the style of setting out from Kumamoto on a day's march to worship at the Kikuchi Shrine, lodging a night at Yamaga and returning. And the students, arms shouldered, would dress their ranks and sing at the top of their voices the Song of the Drawn-Sword Brigade, with words by Toyama Masakazu: "We are the Emperor's army; our foe is the court's enemy whom heaven and earth cannot suffer." Later we came to campaign beyond the prefecture, going as far as the Kagoshima, Saga and Nagasaki directions.

5. Irregular Manoeuvres, Observing the Grand Manoeuvres, and the Like

That among Seiseikō's peculiar forms of training there was something called the irregular manoeuvre also deserves special mention. The irregular manoeuvre was this: with no date fixed at all, the alarm bell would suddenly be beaten wildly in the dead of night to rout the boarders from their beds; the students at once accoutred themselves in straw sandals and gaiters and swiftly fell in before the dormitory. The room that mustered fastest in good order was ranked first; and when the inspection of every room's line was done, we would march some two ri along the midnight roads and return to the dormitory at daybreak. It was training, tempered as a matter of daily habit, in meeting the unforeseen without flurry or fright.

At that period the Vice-Minister of Agriculture and Commerce, Maeda Masana, came to inspect Seiseikō, and happening to glance idly at the dormitory windows, saw straw sandals and gaiters hung without exception at every one. Startled, he asked the reason, was told of the irregular manoeuvres, and was greatly impressed; gathering the whole student body, he touched on the matter in the course of his address and said, "I feel exactly as though I had gone to Germany." That period may fairly be called the very summit of Seiseikō's Spartan education. The exercise did not last, however, and was discontinued around the time I gave up the headship. I note in passing that Maeda Masana had in former years been stationed in Germany as a diplomat, and the era of his residence there was precisely the golden age of the German federation, when the blood-and-iron statesmen, Bismarck, Moltke and the rest, had revived Prussia, defeated Austria, and brought France to a capitulation beneath her own walls.

In Meiji 19 the army held its first manoeuvres pitting brigade against brigade. These manoeuvres set the Kumamoto brigade against the Hiroshima brigade and were conducted on the plain between Chikuzen and Buzen, namely at Shindenbaru. Seiseikō recruited students wishing to observe, and superintendent Okamoto led the party. I set out separately in attendance on Sassa-sensei, and Kumagai Naosuke accompanied us as a correspondent of the Kyūshū Nichinichi Shimbun. On the first day of the manoeuvres the Hiroshima brigade made an opposed landing at the port of Unoshima, and the Kumamoto brigade, resisting for a time, withdrew. The second day was to bring the great decisive battle at Shindenbaru: the Kumamoto army placed its main strength on the level ground of the Bungo highway and lay in wait for the enemy's coming, but the Hiroshima army on the contrary massed its main strength on the army's left wing, that is, toward the hills, and came on at a rush, so that the Kumamoto army, with only the small force of the 13th Regiment, had a thoroughly hard and bitter fight of it. The manoeuvres over, I parted from Sassa-sensei and on the way home, together with the Seiseikō students, crossed near Kosho-san, came out at Akizuki town, and returned to Kumamoto on foot. I note in passing that the commander of the Kumamoto army was Zushi (圖師), the chief of staff Yamaguchi Motoomi (colonel), and the commander of the 13th Regiment Sakamoto Junki (坂本純凞). The others I do not recall.

6. General Nogi and the New Year's Rounds

As I have recorded before, Captain Numata Kuhachirō, instructor of military drill at Seiseikō, was adjutant to Major-General Nogi Maresuke, commander of the Kumamoto brigade. On New Year's Day of Meiji 19 I set out with Minoda Kitarō and Ide Saburō to pay the New Year's rounds at the adjutant's official residence, which stood atop the Hyakken stone rampart of Kumamoto castle. Invited in by Captain Numata, we were drinking toso in an eight-mat room when a voice bellowed at the front entrance, "Is Numata in?" Presently a general officer came tramping in unannounced. He still had his long boots on, spurs and all, and moreover appeared already well in his cups; and this man, who else should he be, was the later war-god, General Nogi. The general plumped himself down before us and pressed the wine cup back and forth again and again. In the end he chanted sonorously a "Poem on New Year's Eve" he said he had composed the night before, and urged us to try chanting it too; nothing loath, we grew merry and chanted along with him. I remember the poem still; its lines run as follows.

A carefree fellow, clumsy in the governance of his kitchen; how often have the dunning devils startled his dreaming soul! And yet he has another stratagem for greeting the year: he has laid in one great cask of army brew.

The chanting of the poem went well enough; what came after was the trouble. The general said, "Students nowadays are cheeky but have no backbone. Can you fellows lift a rice bale clean overhead?" (sashikiru meaning to raise it high above the head with both arms at full stretch). Hating to lose, I flung back flatly, "I can." Then bring a rice bale, it became; inwardly I thought I was in for it now, but by ill luck or by good, the adjutant's residence had no stock of rice bales, and the matter ended there. That there were none was my salvation.

General Nogi, revered today as a god and known as a man of grave and upright rigour, in his prime declined no measure of drink and had, as this shows, his period of unbridled abandon in wine; but the self-cultivation of his later years raised the general at last to divine rank. Truly I believe self-cultivation to be the vital business in which there must be no slackening even to life's final moment.

7. A Call on Yano Fumio

In Meiji 19, when Yano Fumio, returned from Europe, was visiting his home at Saiki town in his native Ōita prefecture, I went to see him at Sassa-sensei's urging, carrying the teacher's letter of introduction and taking Yamada Shuichi with me. At that time Yano, on his return from England, had brought out the celebrated Keikoku Bidan, whose flowing style was on everyone's lips and had raised the price of paper in the capital. He welcomed my call and discoursed to us cordially and in detail on the conditions of England, France and other countries; and it was then, from his talk, that I first learned how ponderous and yet how tenacious the Englishman, that is, the Anglo-Saxon race, is.

8. To the Capital in Sassa-sensei's Train; Inspections along the Way

Before this, that is, in Meiji 17, when Sassa-sensei was going up to the capital, he urged me to accompany him, but the travel money I was to raise myself. After much deliberation I called on Satō Keita at Nakadomi village in Kamoto district and obtained some assistance. I then went down the Kikuchi River by small boat and called on Tomoeda-sensei at Tamana, but as there could be no store of travel subsidies at the teacher's house, I could not bring myself to raise the matter and returned to Kumamoto empty-handed. I remember that after various contrivances I somehow scraped the fare together at last.

The companions of the journey were Uno Shigeru (Tōfū), Haga Gen'iku, Watanabe Saizō and Takeshita Matahei, with Ariyoshi Heikichi, Ariyoshi Chokusan, Inoue Tōjūrō and Hayashida Kiichirō due to join us at Hyakkanseki; today all are gone save Hayashida. The Seiseikō students came as far as Tasaki village to see us off. To go up to the capital in those days one went out from Kumamoto to Hyakkanseki, crossed to Nagasaki, and thence took ship. Leaving Hyakkanseki at half past four in the afternoon, one reached Nagasaki at three the next morning; at four that afternoon we sailed from Nagasaki harbour aboard the Hiroshima-maru, passed two nights on board, and anchored at Kobe at eleven on the morning of the 5th. It sounds like a dream now, but at that time the railway was open only in the Kobe–Osaka quarter, so while we lay at Kobe I took the train with Uno and Hayashida to Osaka and went about seeing the famous places and old sites there. On the 7th at eight in the evening we boarded the Hiroshima-maru again, and on the 9th at ten in the morning landed at Yokohama; between Yokohama and Shimbashi we used the train, and from Shimbashi, by horse tram and rickshaw, arrived at the Yūhi school. The stay in Tokyo ran to seventy days; since the object of Sassa-sensei's journey was to raise an endowment fund for Seiseikō, he spent his time in the capital chiefly in negotiations with high officials and others from our prefecture, and on the homeward road after leaving Tokyo made the rounds of prefecture men in place after place. It was on this journey too that I, introduced by Sassa-sensei, first called on Count Soejima and begged a specimen of his brush. Ever after, each time I went up to the capital, I made it my custom to call on the count and hear his views on politics and much else.

The homeward route was first a night's stop at Hachiōji, another at Saruhashi, and so out to Kōfu; in Yamanashi, Baron Fujimura Shirō was then serving a term as governor. From Kōfu we came to Kajikazawa, went thence by boat down the Fuji River to Iwabuchi, took the Tōkaidō to Hamamatsu to call on Naganuma Tōfu, president of the local district court, and came out at Nagoya. In Nagoya was Yuchi Tsuneo, an official of Aichi prefecture.

From Nagoya we took the road to Nagahama in Ōmi, crossed Lake Biwa by boat and landed at Ōtsu, and then passed through Kyoto and Osaka; from Osaka we again sought passage by sea and landed at the port of Shikama in Hyōgo prefecture. From Shikama we went overland to Himeji, tramped across the Komagaeri Pass and entered the city of Tottori. Since the governor of Tottori was Yamada Nobumichi, and Fukano Ichizō was chief of its police department, we stayed five or six days in Tottori; taking our leave, we crossed the mountain slopes of Inaba and Mimasaka, came out from Chizu to Tsuyama in Mimasaka and went down to Okayama. In Okayama city we visited the Shizutani school, crossed by ship to Takamatsu in Shikoku, and worshipped at the Kotohira Shrine. Stretching our journey to Matsuyama in Iyo, we inspected a school called the Kainan Shoin. Landing from ship at Mitajiri and passing through Yamaguchi, we returned to Kumamoto. Such is the outline of my first journey to the capital, the second of my long tours after the Hichiku ramble.

9. A Letter from Sassa-sensei (the Causes of Germany's Rise)

Dear sirs: at my departure you honoured me by seeing me off despite the distance, for which I am obliged. Last night I arrived at this place. This evening I proceed to Beppu and expect to embark before the night is out. But to the matter: as I told you the other day, for the future of our school there is no way of rearing men and raising the nation but this, to cultivate the spirit of loyalty and patriotism and to bring forth a lively and valorous temper; this was already plain from what I have long lectured, but the other day, from young Kojō's letter, I learned further that herein lies the very seed that has brought Germany's flourishing to pass. At home and abroad the feeling concurs, as tally fits tally. Our school stands apart from the world in a fashion of its own; we must extend the principles we have always professed and, by the accustomed marches and excursions and by various other means, devise how this spirit is to be nourished. I feel those military songs to be needful above all. Indeed, at Mr Kojō's I glanced over Tō's letters: what is sung on German holidays, down to the very girls and children, is without exception loyal and patriotic. He has been translating and sending the songs as well. Accordingly I talked with Mr Kojō of our recent march and the like, and rejoiced greatly that East and West, at home and abroad, are of one mind. On this matter there have also been sundry letters from the Tokyo side, and as I fully intend there shall be a Tokyo present, pray bear this in mind and take care that an atmosphere of the need for military songs, or for singing, be set circulating within the school from this time forward. Are the affairs of the world truly hard to do? It rests only in the man. Gentlemen, strive! In haste, with bowed head.

18 February. Tomofusa. To Brothers Minoda, Adachi and Asayama.

This letter came about thus: after Seiseikō had put military drill into effect and for the first time formed ranks and marched to worship at the Kikuchi Shrine, the teacher set out a few days later for the capital, and at Ōita city (in those days it was reckoned convenient to go overland by rickshaw to Ōita and take ship there) called on Kojō Kamon-sensei (secretary of Ōita prefecture, what would now be a director-general of affairs), perused the letters his son Tō had been sending from Germany, and, finding no way to contain the feelings welling up in him, poured them out to us. One may glimpse from it how immature and how un-national the educational world of our country then was; but more interesting still, the dignity of the letter's language, touching as it does the very root of school management, makes it quite impossible to take for something sent by a principal to a student head. Read it once and you feel it addressed to a vice-principal, or at the least a steward. By this too one may see how deeply we then entered even into the school's administration, and that Mutō-sensei's gibe that the heads were above the stewards was not unprovoked; I set this down here just as memory presents it.

10. To the Capital for Study

By Meiji 20 I was in the Seiseikō boarding house publishing the Daitō Rikkyō Zasshi, frequently begging contributions of Tsuda-sensei as well and serving as its editor. This magazine was later retitled Bungaku Sekai. Through this period I of course remained long in the school boarding house as head, but pondering my own future and looking back over the road I had come, I resolved at last to resign the headship. Consulting with Minoda and Asayama, I decided that the institution of the head student should be abolished with myself as its last holder, and that thereafter all students should be placed under the direct supervision of the dormitory superintendent. I then went on as before, sleeping and rising in a corner of the school in the rented upper storey of the house of Heizō, the caterer, and while tending the publication of Bungaku Sekai set quietly about my plan of going to study in Tokyo. When at length I laid this resolve before Sassa-sensei, he consented gladly and even took thought in various ways for my school expenses in the capital. The upshot was that it was settled I should receive from Baron Fujimura Shirō a stipend of seven yen a month, for one year only. The same provision was made for Asayama Kageharu along with me.

Accordingly Asayama and I at last went up to the capital, settled in at the Tachibanaya, a lodging house in Hongō Daimachi, and promptly enrolled for private instruction in German and set to work. Prices then were, reckoned from today, remarkably cheap, yet on an allowance of seven yen a month it was naturally impossible to cover everything from tuition through lodging. So after much contriving, we six, Yonehara Shigezō, Asayama Kageharu, Jurakuin En, Narauchi Sōtarō, Tomoeda Kyūma (who later reverted to the surname Ichinose) and I, rented a house in Hongō Fukuyamachō and began a communal life, doing our own cooking. Group living makes everything cheaper, and by this we somehow kept body and soul together. The life lasted about a year; but in exchange it was truly riotous and without manners, and though that was an age when the shosei temperament was petted and indulged, in the house next door, one wall away, after the tenants fled, no new tenant could be found for the longest time.
