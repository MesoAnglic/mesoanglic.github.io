Liberal Indo-Optimism

On Bollywood's lost structure of feeling.

2026-03-23(Mon) AST

Disclaimer

I have barely watched Bollywood and I am not Indian. I want to get that out of the way immediately, because I think it matters for what follows. I am an 18-year-old Iraqi who knows a lot about East Asia and who is now, for reasons I will explain, trying to say something about South Asia. If I am wrong, people will tell me why, and I will learn. That is the point.

This article began as a tweet I made in March 2026, reacting to a post by the account @desichad1, who was complaining that a certain class of Bollywood films felt "South Asia and Desi Diaspora coded" and no longer resonated with what he called the "inward-looking and culturally assertive Zoomers of India." I thought he was onto something, but I thought the explanation went deeper than generational vibes. What follows is my attempt to explain why.

The films

Between roughly 2001 and 2014, a set of Hindi-language films emerged that shared a sensibility I have been calling "Liberal Indo-Optimism." The term is mine and I use it loosely, but I think it captures something real. These films are: Dil Chahta Hai (2001), Lagaan (2001), Munna Bhai M.B.B.S. (2003), Swades (2004), Rang De Basanti (2006), Lage Raho Munna Bhai (2006), Chak De! India (2007), Taare Zameen Par (2007), 3 Idiots (2009), My Name is Khan (2010), Zindagi Na Milegi Dobara (2011), OMG – Oh My God! (2012), and PK (2014). You could add Kal Ho Naa Ho (2003), Kabhi Khushi Kabhie Gham (2001), and Hera Pheri (2000) to the periphery. Some of these are comedies, some are dramas, some are SRK vehicles, some are Aamir Khan vehicles, some are Rajkumar Hirani joints. They span genres. But I think they share something.

That something is a set of assumptions about India. India is flawed; its education system is cruel (3 Idiots), its religious institutions are superstitious (PK, OMG), its villages lack electricity (Swades), its nationalism can be exclusionary (Chak De! India). But these flaws are reformable. They can be fixed through individual goodwill, institutional critique, cosmopolitan openness, and a composite understanding of Indian identity that includes Hindus, Muslims, Christians, and everyone else. The protagonist of Chak De! India is a Muslim hockey coach who has to prove his patriotism by coaching the women's team. The protagonist of PK is an alien who falls in love with the idea that an Indian girl can date a Pakistani boy. The protagonist of Swades is an NRI scientist who leaves NASA to bring electricity to a rural village. The protagonist of My Name is Khan is a Muslim man in post-9/11 America who tells the President that his name is Khan and he is not a terrorist. These are progressive films, in a general sense; they assume India is getting better, and that the trajectory of liberalisation, globalisation, and social reform will continue.

The commonality of these films, political or otherwise, is that they are expressions of something that now feels very dated in the modern India. It is no coincidence that most of them are from the Manmohan Singh era.

Why they existed: the multiplex and the boom

The anthropologist Tejaswini Ganti, in her 2012 book Producing Bollywood, offers the structural explanation for why these films could exist in the first place. After India's economic liberalisation in 1991, the media landscape transformed. Satellite television arrived, and then the multiplex theatre. The multiplex changed the economics of Hindi cinema fundamentally. Filmmakers no longer needed to appeal to the mass audience of single-screen cinemas across the Hindi heartland to turn a profit. Higher ticket prices in urban multiplexes, combined with the lucrative overseas diaspora market in the UK and North America, meant that a film could recover its costs from fewer people paying more money. Ganti calls this process the "gentrification" of Hindi cinema. The filmmakers of this era were themselves second- and third-generation industry figures from upper-middle-class, often English-educated backgrounds. With the economic pressure to cater to mass audiences relaxed, they began making films that reflected their own sensibilities; cosmopolitan, reformist, socially liberal.

This is the crucial point. The Liberal Indo-Optimist films were produced by and for the Indian urban middle class during a period of extraordinary economic confidence. India was posting 8–9% GDP growth. The middle class was expanding. Liberalisation felt like it was working. The films did not create this mood; they reflected it. They were the aesthetic expression of a set of material conditions that happened to coincide with the United Progressive Alliance government of Manmohan Singh (2004–2014), but were caused by the broader post-liberalisation boom rather than by any particular political party.

Girish Shahane, writing in The Caravan in 2011, made a pointed observation. In Ketan Mehta's Holi (1984), college students riot against an authoritarian educational establishment and end up in jail. In Rajkumar Hirani's 3 Idiots (2009), the protagonists reach amicable resolutions with authority and move on to flourishing careers. The shift in mood between the two films relates directly to the improved prospects for graduates in liberalised India. The multiplex audience, young urban professionals with disposable income and a sense of upward mobility, did not want searching inquiries into the structures of Indian society. They wanted to feel good about a country that was, for once, visibly getting richer. Hirani was the master of this: from Munna Bhai M.B.B.S. through PK, his films attacked middle-class anxieties (corruption, superstition, educational pressure) while resolving them through individual charm, moral courage, and the fundamental decency of ordinary Indians. A ThePrint profile aptly called him Bollywood's "middle-class reformer." The audiences loved it, and the films made enormous amounts of money.

The turn

I want to be careful here, because the periodization is messier than my original tweets implied. When I first posted about this, a friend of mine who goes by Zhang Xueliang online (he is an Indian right-winger who uses the name of a Chinese warlord as his persona, for reasons I have never fully understood) pushed back hard. His argument was that the genre I was describing actually peaked during Modi's first term, not during UPA, and that I should look at any Akshay Kumar movie between 2014 and COVID. He is largely right. Films like Toilet: Ek Prem Katha (2017) and Pad Man (2018) carried the reformist-optimist sensibility well into the BJP era. Dangal (2016), an Aamir Khan film about women's wrestling that was enormously popular in China as well, has the progressive gender politics and developmental nationalism that would fit comfortably in my corpus. The sensibility did not die on election night 2014.

Zhang also pointed out that militaristic, hawkish Indian cinema existed during the supposed liberal era; Sarfarosh (1999) and the film 16 December (2002) are examples. The clean binary of "UPA = liberal cinema, Modi = nationalist cinema" does not survive contact with the actual filmography. What I am tracking is a dominant mode that coexists with other modes in every period. The question is what is prestigious and culturally central, what defines the era's self-image.

And on that question, I think the shift is real, even if the timeline is blurrier than I initially claimed. The UPA-era optimism carried into Modi's first term (2014–2019), but it began fraying, and by the second term it was largely gone. The CAA protests, the effects of COVID, and the broader hardening of Indian political culture changed the register. What replaced it was not simply a more pessimistic cinema; it was a cinema with a different object of pride. Films like URI: The Surgical Strike (2019), The Kashmir Files (2022), and the historical epics about Hindu warrior kings (Tanhaji, Samrat Prithviraj, Chhaava) express a nationalism that is civilisational rather than composite. The pride is specifically Hindu, the enemies are specifically Muslim (or Pakistani, or secular-liberal), and the state is an instrument of strength rather than reform.

A SOAS analysis of this trend put it sharply: the BJP has succeeded in "saffronising" Bollywood by influencing market forces to make Hindutva stories more profitable and by marginalising dissenting voices, rather than by converting the industry's largely apolitical members into Hindu nationalists.

Both Chak De! India and URI are attempts at expressing Indian nationalism. But they do it in ways that are deeply reminiscent of their respective eras. In Chak De!, the nationalism is composite: a Muslim man proves his loyalty to India through an inclusive, multi-ethnic women's team. In URI, the nationalism is civilisational: loud chants of Jai Hind, the pride of India located in the air force, the Muslim enemy defeated. PK is literally about dating a Pakistani. That film cannot be made in today's India; the fact that it ever was is, in retrospect, surprising.

Structure of feeling

The cultural theorist Raymond Williams coined the term "structure of feeling" in Marxism and Literature (1977) to describe something that is not quite ideology, not quite mood, but a shared sensibility that is historically situated and that pervades the cultural production of a particular period. I think this is the right frame for what I am describing. Liberal Indo-Optimism was not a political programme. The filmmakers were not Congress partisans. They were riding a mood; a structure of feeling that emerged from the post-liberalisation economic boom, from the expansion of the middle class, from the sense that India was arriving on the world stage as a globalised, pluralist, upwardly-mobile democracy. The films assumed that this trajectory would continue indefinitely. They did not just "date"; the world they assumed ceased to exist.

I said in a conversation with a friend that I think Tocqueville is relevant here. In Democracy in America, Tocqueville observes that as social conditions improve, social tensions can actually increase, because expectations rise faster than reality can satisfy them. I think this maps onto post-liberalisation India. The significant economic growth from the 1991 liberalisation (initiated, incidentally, by Manmohan Singh as Finance Minister) generated enormous returns for many Indians, but those returns were unevenly distributed and the promise of continued growth became harder to sustain. For the middle class, as economic concerns became relatively less pressing, social and cultural concerns became relatively more important. This helped the BJP, whose appeal is precisely in the domain of cultural identity rather than economic policy. Indian nationalism in the current era is, in a sense, the nationalism of a middle class whose material needs have been partially met and who now seek meaning in civilisational pride and cultural assertion. And you can track this through Bollywood.

From the outside

Priyank, a verified Indian account who replied to my tweet, said something that stuck with me: "If you dig further you'll find a cultural current which shows up in politics but is deeper than it. It's about how to balance fantasy and realism." I think that is a better formulation than mine. The Liberal Indo-Optimist films were fantasies, in the sense that they imagined an India that was always on the verge of becoming its best self. The post-2019 films are also fantasies, but of a different kind: fantasies of a civilisational greatness that was stolen and must be reclaimed. Both are responses to the same underlying reality; a country that is too large, too complex, and too contradictory to match any single cinematic vision of itself.

I am writing about this as a mleccha, to use the Sanskrit term I half-jokingly applied to myself on Twitter. I know more about India than most foreigners, but that is a low bar. What I think I can offer is the perspective of someone who watches these films from outside the Indian political context entirely, and who can therefore notice things that might be too obvious or too politically charged for an insider to articulate comfortably. When I say that Chak De! India and URI: The Surgical Strike are both attempts at Indian nationalism that do it in very different ways reminiscent of their respective eras, an Indian nationalist might take offence at the comparison, and an Indian liberal might take offence at calling Chak De! nationalist at all. I have no horse in that race. I am just watching the films.

What I see is a country whose cinema tracked, with remarkable fidelity, a shift in collective self-understanding. From a pluralist, reformist, cosmopolitan optimism that believed India was on its way to becoming something new; to a civilisational, assertive, inward-looking pride that believes India must reclaim something old. Whether you think this shift is a tragedy or a correction probably depends on your politics. But the cinema doesn't lie. The films that a society makes, and the films that a society stops making, tell you what that society believes about itself. And the fact that PK, a film about an alien who thinks humans should date across the India-Pakistan border, was the highest-grossing Indian film of 2014, and that The Kashmir Files, a film about Hindu suffering at the hands of Muslims, was one of the biggest cultural events of 2022, tells you something important about what changed in between.

I don't know exactly what. I am 18 and I live in Baghdad. But I think the question is worth asking, and I think the films are worth watching, and I think the answer is somewhere in the gap between Swades and URI; between a nationalism that lights a bulb in a village and a nationalism that enters your home and kills you.
